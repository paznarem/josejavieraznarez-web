About Me

My story

A human being walking alongside other human beings

My path: learning to move forward in difficult moments

For over thirty-five years, I have supported people in moments when moving forward was far from obvious. Along this journey — supporting, guiding, and mentoring families, children, and adolescents through their development, growth, and everyday difficulties — I have learned to observe what helps people keep going when everything gets complicated.

This is not just about accumulated experience, but about the context in which I have been able to see, first-hand, how human responses are shaped in real life.

Throughout my life, I have built my own rules and behavioural patterns through learning in very diverse contexts. I have had the privilege of being a son, a brother in a large family, a husband, a father of three children, and recently, a grandfather.

Each of these roles has given me experiences that have shaped my capacity to love, hold, listen, understand, and support — not only on a personal level, but also in my work as a psychologist.

There was a key moment in my story that still directly influences how I work today:

My path began as a professional musician during adolescence. It was not just an artistic journey, but ongoing training in something very specific: continuing to move forward in what mattered to me amid pressure, evaluation, and difficulty.

Balancing music with academic studies placed me, from an early age, in a situation I now recognise as central to my work: doing what mattered while errors, doubts, activation, and moments of paralysis kept appearing.

That process was training me — without me realising it at the time — in a very specific direction: not depending on how I felt in order to act, and sustaining my behaviour even when my inner experience was pushing in the opposite direction.

I did not just learn responsibility or perseverance there. I learned something more precise: to keep acting even when I did not feel ready, to deal with frustration without abandoning what I was doing, and not to wait until everything felt right inside before taking the next step.

Over time, I have recognised that same pattern in many of the people I support: moments when anxiety, insecurity, or self-criticism begin to organise what they do and limit their lives.

And that is precisely where I work today: not in eliminating that inner experience, but in helping the person respond differently when it appears, expanding their room for action in the areas that matter to them.

That learning — moving forward with difficulty present, without letting what is inside set the limits of action — remains the core of my work today.

And, at the same time, that journey has not stopped there.

Alongside the accumulated experience, I have maintained a rigorous and demanding continuing education, not focused on adding techniques, but on understanding precisely which processes are at play when a person gets stuck, and what conditions allow those patterns to change.

During my long years as a student — which are still not over — I have developed a way of understanding human behaviour based on functional analysis: what happens, in what context, with what function, and what consequences maintain what is currently limiting you.

Why this matters in my work as a psychologist

I do not start only from theoretical training, but from having spent decades in direct contact with people in real situations: in the classroom, in the family, at work, in relationships, on stage, in moments of conflict, growth, and difficulty.

This allows me to go beyond general explanations and understand how what is happening to you takes shape in your everyday life: what triggers it, what maintains it, and why, despite your efforts, it keeps repeating itself.

My mistakes, my strength

I have made mistakes and taken decisions with unexpected outcomes, some of them painful. Perhaps more than successes.

I have learned, gradually, to recognise those moments as part of my story — without needing to erase them or fight against them, but understanding them as part of a process from which one also learns.

Today I try to live with attention to the present, acknowledging my worries, fears, and moments of sadness as part of the human experience. Not so they disappear, but so they do not automatically drive what I do.

It is in challenges that I find the opportunity to act in line with what matters to me.

My work as a psychologist

As a contextual psychologist, my approach moves away from the idea that there is something to "fix" or "cure" in the person.

I do not work from labels, but from the analysis of what is happening in your life.

My work focuses on understanding how your responses are organised in specific situations: what happens before, what you do, and what short- and long-term consequences it carries.

Not as isolated elements, but as patterns that have been built throughout your history and that still operate today, even though sometimes at a high cost.

I aim to help you identify those patterns and, above all, open up alternatives when those ways of responding no longer bring you closer to the life you want to live.

It is not about applying techniques or changing what you feel.

It is about no longer getting trapped in those automatic responses and regaining the capacity to choose what you do, even when distress is present.

Rather than focusing on symptoms, the goal is for you to respond more flexibly to what is happening, moving forward — with everything that shows up — toward a life more consistent with what matters to you.

This is what I offer: a space to understand precisely what is happening to you and to open up real alternatives for responding differently.

My commitment to you

Your mountain. Your journey.

Your life is a journey of your own, with obstacles, decisions, and moments when moving forward is far from obvious.

My role is not to set the path or tell you what you should do.

It is to help you see more clearly where you are, what is influencing how you move forward, and what options are available to you at each moment.

From my experience — after more than thirty-five years supporting people in real-life contexts — I can offer you a perspective grounded in observation, analysis, and direct work.

I am not here to judge you.

I am here to help you understand and to act.

The goal is not to create dependence or to extend the process unnecessarily.

It is for you to regain the autonomy to respond to what is happening with greater flexibility and direction.

My role is to walk alongside you.

Change is built in what you do with what happens to you.

My experience as a father

Raising a child is one of the most exciting and, at the same time, most demanding tasks there is.

I have been there too.

I believed that love, dedication, and responsibility would be enough. And yet, there were moments when I did not understand what was happening or how to respond differently.

I made mistakes — some avoidable, others not — always from a place of commitment.

Over time, I came to understand something important: we do not always need to do better; sometimes we need to better understand what is happening and what function our actions serve.

If I had had that framework earlier, it would have spared me many doubts and moments of guilt.

That experience is not a limitation in my work.

It is part of its foundation.

Today I support mothers and fathers not from theory, but from having been there myself — and from understanding, with greater precision, what happens in the relationship when things get complicated.
