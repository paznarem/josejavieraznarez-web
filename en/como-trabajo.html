How I Work

How I Work

Contextual, personalised, and evidence-based support

My approach

I am a psychologist specialising in contextual therapies. I work with people who, despite their efforts, feel trapped in anxiety, distress, or recurring patterns of response.

There is something important to understand from the start: what is happening to you makes sense given your history and the context you live in, and many of the strategies you use provide short-term relief but, over time, can limit your life.

In session, we do not seek to label or diagnose; instead, we analyse what is happening in your daily life and what maintains it, so you can respond more flexibly even when distress is still present.

I work with you so that you can:

  • Understand what you do when you feel bad and why it persists
  • Stop getting trapped by thoughts, emotions, or automatic reactions
  • Act more consistently with what matters to you, even when distress shows up
  • Regain direction and room for action in your life

I work from an evidence-based contextual approach, focused on psychological processes that have been shown to be key to change.

It is not about eliminating what you feel, but about learning to relate to it in a way that does not limit your life.

How I can help you

I work with people whose distress is beginning to influence the way they live, make decisions, and relate to others. We do not address isolated "problems," but rather response patterns that have been built up over time and that now limit your life.

These are some of the most common situations I work with:

Anxiety and sustained activation

When your alarm system stays switched on — constant worry, anticipation, tension — and your behaviour starts to organise itself around avoiding, controlling, or reducing that activation.

We work so that you can act with greater freedom even when activation is present, expanding your range of response rather than shrinking your life.

Self-criticism, shame, and rigid self-evaluation

When the way you talk to yourself, compare yourself, or evaluate yourself starts to drive what you do, limiting your behaviour and narrowing your options.

We intervene on how those judgments function in your behaviour, so they stop governing your decisions and you can move with greater psychological flexibility.

Experiential avoidance, over-control, and paralysis

When you try not to feel, control what is happening inside, or do everything "perfectly," and that ends up generating more rigidity, more exhaustion, or more paralysis.

We analyse what function that control serves in your context and develop alternatives that allow you to act without depending on eliminating what you feel.

Sadness, disconnection, and inactivity

When discouragement, apathy, or loss of energy reduce your activity and pull you away from what is important to you.

We work on the progressive recovery of movement and contact with activities that can restore energy and direction, without forcing it or waiting to "feel better" before starting.

Relationship difficulties

When repeated conflicts, misunderstandings, distance, or interaction patterns recur and deteriorate the relationship.

We intervene on how you respond in those contexts, identifying interaction loops (attack, defence, withdrawal, over-adaptation) and expanding more attuned ways of relating.

Decision-making, life direction, and paralysis

When you constantly doubt, postpone decisions, or feel you have lost direction.

We work to clarify what is important to you in practice and to act in that direction, even amid uncertainty or fear.

Parenting support

For mothers and fathers who want to understand what is happening in the relationship with their children and respond more effectively, even in moments of high activation.

We work on co-regulation, boundaries in context, functional analysis of what is happening at home, and ways of responding that nurture the relationship without losing direction.

Performance anxiety and exposure (musicians, artists, professionals)

When activation shows up in evaluation or exposure situations — on stage, in exams, during presentations — and starts to interfere with your performance.

The work does not focus on reducing nerves, but on relating differently to that activation so you can perform with presence, stability, and effectiveness.

If you do not see yourself reflected exactly in these eight areas, we can assess your situation individually.

What sessions look like

Each person arrives with a different history and with ways of responding that have been shaped by their context. That is why the work is always tailored to what is happening for you: there are no recipes, no rigid protocols, no fixed instructions.

The process is organised around three principles:

What you feel makes sense in your history

Difficult emotions — fear, sadness, insecurity, uncertainty — are not errors or signs that something is wrong with you. They are responses that have developed in your history and serve a function. The first step is not to eliminate them, but to understand how they operate in your current life.

What you do is not random: it serves a function

Your ways of responding — avoiding, controlling, demanding of yourself, disconnecting — do not appear at random. They have been useful at some point and that is why they persist. In session, we analyse what function they serve today, what their short- and long-term consequences are, and what room there is to respond differently.

Change is oriented toward how you want to live

The goal is not to feel better so you can start living, but to be able to live differently even when distress shows up. We work to clarify what is important in your life and develop ways of acting that are more consistent with it, expanding your options rather than reducing them.

In practice

Each session is an active workspace. We talk about what is happening to you in specific situations. We analyse what happens before, what you do, and what happens afterwards. We observe what maintains it in the present. We try out new ways of responding in session. Not from theory, but from what is happening here and now.

Scientific framework

My work is grounded in Contextual Behavioral Science (CBS), an approach that studies how what you think, feel, and do is shaped by your history and the context you live in.

From this framework, models such as Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), Functional Analytic Psychotherapy (FAP), and Relational Frame Theory (RFT) have been developed, alongside other approaches such as Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), Behavioral Activation (BA), Compassion-Focused Therapy (CFT), Integrative Behavioral Couple Therapy in its contextual and functional version, and Mindful Self-Compassion (MSC) programmes.

All of them can be integrated from the same foundation: analysing what is happening in your case, what maintains it, and what can be changed in the context you live in.

Rather than focusing on labels or applying specific techniques, the work is oriented toward understanding how your response patterns function and expanding your options so you can act more flexibly.

This approach aligns with the current developments of Process-Based Therapy (PBT), which allows the work to be tailored to each person with precision. This means I do not apply a particular therapy; instead, I work on what is happening in your case and how it is maintained.

Well-being and distress — a different way of understanding them

Well-being

It does not mean the absence of pain. It means the capacity to live a meaningful life in harmony with your values, despite the inevitable presence of painful thoughts, difficult emotions, and adverse circumstances. It involves fully accepting your internal experiences and committing to actions that reflect what is truly important to you.

Distress

It is not what you feel, but what happens when you struggle against what you feel. When you try to control, avoid, or suppress pain, fear, or uncertainty, that resistance consumes energy and pulls you away from the life you want to live. Suffering does not come from pain itself — it comes from the futile struggle against it.

This distinction is central to my work. I do not ask you to stop feeling distress. I support you in changing your relationship with it, so you can act in the direction you choose, even when difficult emotions are present.

What this therapy is NOT

I want to be clear about what I offer and what I do not:

  • It is not clinical or healthcare services. I do not diagnose or treat psychological disorders or pathologies. My work falls under psychological consulting and support, in accordance with Spanish Law 44/2003 on the Regulation of Health Professions.
  • It is not the same as seeing a psychiatrist or clinical psychologist. These professionals are responsible for diagnosing and treating mental illness, often with pharmacological interventions. My approach is different and complementary.
  • It is not open-ended therapy. It is oriented toward resolving difficulties and overcoming barriers within a reasonable timeframe, avoiding dependence on prolonged processes.
  • It is not a motivational talk or an advice session. It is a structured teaching and learning process, grounded in scientific evidence, where you practise new behaviours and I guide you through that process.

If your situation requires clinical or healthcare attention, I will tell you honestly and refer you to the appropriate professional.

In each session, we explore together the private events you experience: intrusive thoughts, intense emotions, impulses, and physical sensations. Rather than fighting against them or trying to suppress them, we learn to make room for them and to understand the function they serve in your daily life.

We work on self-knowledge in action: observing with curiosity and without judgment how your internal language, beliefs, and behavioural patterns relate to what is happening around you. We also explore bodily sensations — tension, fatigue, palpitations — as valuable information connected to your emotions, not as enemies to be eliminated.

We identify your strengths, values, and genuine desires — not the ones your environment expects of you — and use them as a compass. We analyse the functional meaning of each behaviour: which patterns still work, which ones pull you away from the life you want, and what alternatives you can build step by step.

The result is not a to-do list, but your own repertoire of tools for responding with greater flexibility and coherence, session by session, at your own pace.
