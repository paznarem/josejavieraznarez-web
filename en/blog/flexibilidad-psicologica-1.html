When giving up trying to feel better lets you start living better

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 12 min read

← Back to blog

There is a very specific moment that happens many times a day. You're about to do something important. A conversation. A decision. A step you've been putting off for some time.

And something shows up inside: discomfort, doubt, a thought: "not now," "better tomorrow."

And without realising it, you've already changed direction. Not because you don't know what to do. But because something inside you has taken over the deciding.

Central concept

That is the point where psychological flexibility is at stake: the capacity to sustain or change what you do in line with what matters to you, even when difficult thoughts, emotions or sensations show up.

What you've already tried (and why it isn't enough)

If you're honest, this isn't new. You've tried to feel better. To think differently. To control what you feel. To avoid what hurts. To wait until you were ready.

And many times it has worked. In the short term. It has calmed you. It has protected you. It has let you carry on.

But there's a more important question: what has been happening in the meantime? Not as a reproach. As observation. Are there decisions still waiting? Conversations you haven't had? Moments when relief arrived, but what you wanted to do stayed undone?

✦ How it shows up

This takes different forms in different people. For some it's work: they know what they have to do, but they put it off indefinitely. For others it's relational: they stay silent when they'd like to speak, or speak when they'd like to stay silent. For others it's the body: they read every physical signal as a threat and organise the day around not getting activated.

The content varies. The structure is the same. And recognising which one is yours is the first real step.

This doesn't happen through lack of capacity. It happens because distress has become the criterion. And that has a clear consequence: your life starts organising itself around not feeling bad.

The point where this stops being a solution

What you do in order not to feel bad is limiting the life you want to live. What's difficult is that many of those strategies are reasonable. But that doesn't stop them, together, from shrinking your life.

The more you try not to feel something, the more it shapes what you do. The more you need to be okay before you can act, the less you act. And that leaves you stuck in a constant wait: waiting to be okay in order to start living. That is the turning point.

The hopelessness that opens a path

There is a moment when something becomes evident. It isn't pleasant. What you recognise is not that you haven't tried enough. It's that the whole system you've been using — controlling what you feel in order to live — can't give you what you're looking for. Not because you've done it wrong. But because that's not how it works: distress doesn't get controlled enough, and the more you try, the more ground it takes.

Key concept

That recognition has a name: creative hopelessness. And it is creative precisely because it isn't a defeat. It's the moment when you stop spending energy in a direction that leads nowhere, and you can start looking somewhere else.

The shift of axis

Psychological flexibility isn't about eliminating distress. It's about something more demanding: no longer organising your behaviour around what you feel and starting to organise it around what matters.

This doesn't tidy up your mind. It doesn't automatically lower anxiety. But it changes something crucial: you no longer need all of that to disappear in order to act.

Letting what is there, be

Before you can move in another direction, there is something prior that many people skip: letting the experience be there.

Not resolving it. Not explaining it. Not rushing it away. Simply noticing that there is anxiety, or sadness, or doubt, and not immediately launching a campaign to make it disappear.

This is not resignation, nor is it tolerance. Tolerating is gritting your teeth and waiting for it to pass. What happens here is different: it is actively making room for what is happening, without that space being a threat. Not because the experience is pleasant, but because fighting against it costs more than letting it affect you.

Key concept

In Acceptance and Commitment Therapy, this is what is called acceptance: not passivity, but an active choice to make room for what is there so that your energy can go to what matters. It is a choice, not a surrender. And like every choice, it has to be repeated.

When that happens — not always, not all at once, but when it happens — the inner experience loses part of its urgency. It doesn't disappear, but it no longer demands to be resolved before you can do anything.

Relating differently to what you think

When a thought shows up like "I can't," "this will go badly" or "better not," you don't need to argue with it. You can notice something different: "I'm having the thought that I can't." The content doesn't change. But something important does: how it acts on you.

Words have a curious property: they can move us even when we know they're only words. It's the same mechanism by which you can salivate thinking about a lemon, or feel vertigo imagining a height. It isn't irrationality. It's how language works in us.

Thoughts don't move us because they're true. They move us because they're there, active, and we follow them automatically. When we follow them this way, without any distance, they act as commands: that is what is called cognitive fusion. When you notice that you are having a thought, you introduce a small distance between the thought and what you do with it. It stops working as an automatic command and becomes something you can have without obeying. That movement — creating distance between the thought and the action — is what is called cognitive defusion. The space is small. But it's enough to choose.

And if you look more closely, something else happens. Thoughts change. Emotions change. Sensations come and go. But the fact that you can notice all of that — that you're reading this now and being aware of what's happening inside — that doesn't change.

It isn't a separate entity. It isn't a "true self" hidden behind everything else. It is simply the perspective from which everything is happening. The place from which you can say "this is happening to me" instead of "this is who I am." That perspective is what is called self-as-context: not an observer that judges, but the place from which you observe. When that perspective is available, what happens inside stops defining you and becomes something that is happening in you. And from there, choosing is possible even if the experience doesn't change.

Coming back to the present

Without the constant struggle to control the inner world, something we usually avoid appears: the present moment. Not the ideal one. Not the one that should be. This one. With discomfort. With doubts. With activation. And even so, available. That present-moment awareness — being here, with what there is, without needing it to be different — is where you can act. Not when you're ready. Now.

The question that changes everything

At this point, the question is no longer "how do I get rid of this?" It's: "what kind of life do I want to build, even with this present?" Not as a general idea. As an immediate criterion. What matters in this conversation? What kind of decisions do you want to make today?

Three levels that matter

For those questions to have a concrete answer, it helps to distinguish three things that are often confused. A value is a chosen direction: "I want to live my relationship here and now." It isn't a preference or a wish: it is a direction that still makes sense even if you don't obtain the result. A goal is a concrete outcome: "I'm going to call that person this week." A committed action is what you do right now: "I dial the number."

Without the direction, the action makes no sense. Without the action, the direction is just an idea. The three levels matter, and all three are yours.

How you treat yourself when you don't manage it

There's something that blocks this process without being noticed: how you treat yourself when you avoid, when you react, when you fall back into the same old pattern.

If "here we go again," "you never change" or "you should be able to" show up, you aren't helping. You're adding internal threat right when you need room. The key move is to treat yourself in a way that doesn't increase the struggle. Not to feel better, but to be able to keep going: "this is hard," "it makes sense that it's costly," "I can come back from here." This isn't indulgence. It's creating the conditions in which change is possible.

A minimal example

✦ In practice

You have to make a decision you've been avoiding for some time. Anxiety shows up. Doubts. The urge to postpone.

Before: you avoid, you wait.

Now: you notice there is anxiety; you notice you are having the thought that this isn't the moment; you let that be without turning it into a command; and you ask: "If this weren't deciding for me, what would I do now?" And you take a step.

Small, yes. But not isolated. Because psychological flexibility is not defined by single decisions, but by the repetition of choices in a direction. Today you make that call. Tomorrow you hold that conversation. Another day you try again. Not because it's easy, but because it matters. That's what turns an action into commitment: not what you do once, but what you keep doing when it isn't easy.

Closing

Psychological flexibility doesn't eliminate distress. Nor does it promise that it will stop showing up or lose all its intensity. What changes is its function: instead of acting as a stop sign, it can become a sign that you're doing something that matters. Distress is still there. But it is no longer the referee.

When distress stops being the referee, life stops organising itself around avoiding what hurts and starts organising itself around what matters. And that is the point where change stops being an idea and starts being a way of living.

It is built with these movements, again and again: notice · let be · return · choose · act.

Not always in that order. What matters isn't the sequence. It is that those movements keep appearing, in one form or another, in the direction of what matters. Not as perfection, but as practice. It isn't easier. But it is more livable.

Not to feel better before living. But to start living, even if you don't quite feel okay yet.
