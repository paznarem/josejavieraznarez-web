Our baby is here. Co-sleeping?

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 10 min read

← Back to blog

This is the first article in the series Co-sleeping — Parenting with direction. Across the upcoming articles we'll walk through the three stages of co-sleeping in the early years, the caregiver's emotional environment, and the myths about dependence and limits.

The first three years of a baby's life are a crucial period for the development of the bond, emotion regulation, and the child's wellbeing. Many caregivers, especially first-time ones, face huge pressure to do things "right" or "perfectly," which fuels insecurity about how to raise their child. During these years, one of the topics that raises the most doubts is co-sleeping: sleeping next to the baby.

If you are reading this with a baby in your arms, or with your phone in your hand at three in the morning, or simply with that nagging feeling that you should be doing better: this article is for you. Not for the ideal mother. For you, exactly as you are right now.

Maybe you're not alone in bed, but it feels that way. Maybe there's a partner beside you who doesn't get it, or who does get it but is also exhausted, or with whom you haven't really talked for weeks because between the baby and the tiredness there's nothing left. Maybe there are days when you don't recognise yourself: you don't recognise your body, your desire, or that sadness with no clear name that's there every morning. Maybe you feel ashamed of not enjoying motherhood more, or afraid that something in you is failing in a way that has nothing to do with co-sleeping but with everything else.

This article cannot solve that. But it can tell you that the weight you carry — the partner, the sadness, the fear, the loneliness inside your own home — does not make you a bad mother. It makes you a person trying to care for another human being while she herself needs care. That is not a character flaw. It is the most demanding situation that exists, and very few people name it honestly.

Although co-sleeping has been a traditional practice in many cultures throughout history, in Western societies it tends to be seen as controversial. Caregivers look for information online, ask family members or healthcare professionals, and often run into contradictory opinions. Will the child become too dependent? Will they have trouble sleeping alone? Will it affect the couple relationship?

Many families who practise co-sleeping notice, over time, that their child does not become more dependent but more able to self-soothe, to explore, to separate without distress. This doesn't happen by chance: co-sleeping, practised consciously and consistently, can support the child gradually developing the capacity to regulate themselves.

An evolutionary context

For thousands of years, in nearly every human culture, babies have slept next to their caregivers. Not because there was no other option, but because that proximity served functions that evolution selected for: protection, temperature regulation, access to feeding, contact.

The Extended Evolutionary Meta-Model (EEMM) — a framework that understands human behavior as shaped at once by biological evolution, cultural history, and each person's personal history — lets us understand co-sleeping precisely from there: as a behavior the species kept because it protected and regulated.

Three levels at once

Analyzed through the EEMM, co-sleeping operates simultaneously on three levels. In the baby's body: regulation of temperature, heart rate, breathing. In their emotional and relational development: the gradual building of the capacity to regulate and to trust. And in the culture of care that each family builds and passes on. Three levels that don't follow one another but happen at the same time, every night, in the same bed.

Conscious physical and emotional co-sleeping

Any caregiver who has practised co-sleeping with attention will have noticed something: the baby who at first only calms down with direct physical contact gradually becomes able to calm down with just the voice, with closeness, with the simple certainty that someone is there.

This is not by chance. What is happening, according to the principles of Contextual Behavioral Science (CBS) — an approach within scientific psychology that studies human behavior always in relation to the context in which it occurs — is a layered process worth unpacking.

The starting point is simple: each time the caregiver responds consistently to the baby's crying, learning takes place. Not one-off learning, but accumulated history. That mechanism — the relation between what the baby does (crying, seeking) and what happens next (the caregiver shows up, soothes, holds) — is called a contingency of reinforcement. It is basic learning: behavior that obtains a response tends to consolidate.

Technical note RFT and relational frames: how calmness generalises

Over time, that accumulated history of presence and response begins to function as something more than direct learning. The baby starts to connect things they haven't directly experienced together: the caregiver's voice with the calm that previously only came from physical contact, closeness with the safety that previously required an embrace. Without anyone explaining it, the baby begins to derive — to extract on their own — that the caregiver's availability still exists even when contact is not so immediate. This phenomenon, by which words, ideas, and experiences connect with one another and properties transfer across them — the voice "inheriting" the calm of the embrace — is called, in this approach, a relational frame of coordination. Relational Frame Theory (RFT) studies precisely how we learn to connect words, ideas, and experiences with one another, and how those connections affect us even when we haven't lived them directly.

And there's something even more precise. Among the networks of connections the baby is building, some are especially important: those that allow them to distinguish what is happening to them from what is happening to the other, what is happening here from what is happening there, what is happening now from what happened before or will happen later. These distinctions — between I and you, here and there, now and then — are the foundation on which much of the child's emotional life is built. They are called deictic relational frames, and they are the scaffolding from which the child will learn to observe what they feel without being trapped by it.

This process, when it develops consistently, supports something with a technical name and a very concrete translation. What the baby gradually gains is the ability to calm down not only with the caregiver's body, but also with their voice, and later with the memory of that voice, and later still with the inner certainty that the caregiver exists and is available even when not in sight. That — emotion regulation moving from physical contact to language — is called verbally mediated regulation. And it always happens gradually and respectfully of the child's pace.

What the baby learns each time the caregiver responds to their call is not just that this adult is there: they learn that they can count on them. That the signal of distress has a response. That the connection does not disappear.

That accumulated history — of presence, of voice, of contact, of validation — is what Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) describes as committed action in line with values: caring not because it is easy or because it comes out perfect, but because it is what matters most. And it is precisely that history that, over time, allows the child to develop a sense of safety that does not depend exclusively on the caregiver being physically present.
