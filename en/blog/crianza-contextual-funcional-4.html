What happens to you when your child cries: the processes that organize your responses

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 19 min read

In the previous articles, Contextual Functional Parenting was introduced as a framework that analyzes processes, not categories; we explained how the parent-child relationship works as a bidirectional system; and we showed how everyday language shapes the way the child relates to themselves. This fourth article reaches the core of the framework: the concrete psychological processes that organize what you do —or cannot do— in the most difficult moments of parenting. It is not about learning new techniques, but about understanding what is happening inside you when your capacity to care gets blocked.

The processes that organize how we respond

Psychological flexibility, as defined by Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), is the capacity to be in contact with what is happening right now, to observe your own experience —thoughts, emotions, sensations— without becoming trapped in it, and to act in the direction of what really matters to you, even when that experience is uncomfortable. In the context of CFP, this concept goes beyond the individual: the caregiving adult's psychological flexibility is also a condition of the environment the child needs in order to develop their own. The child learns to be psychologically flexible in large part because the adult who is parenting them is psychologically flexible in their presence.

This concept —psychological flexibility— is central to everything that follows and is one of those that most differentiates this approach from others. It is not the same as «resilience» (returning to a previous state after a difficulty) or «emotional intelligence» (a concept that tends to treat emotions as things to be managed from above). Psychological flexibility is something more precise: being able to act from your values even when your thoughts, emotions, and sensations are pushing in another direction.

It is composed of six interrelated processes. We describe them one by one, but in practice they act together.

Present-moment awareness

The adult who can stay in contact with what is happening —the child's facial expression, the tone of their voice, the quality of their bodily movement, the texture of the interaction in real time— has access to information that the adult trapped in rules about «how this should be» cannot process. That attunement, which attachment studies consider a key predictor of development, in practice consists of knowing how to connect with what is happening in the «here and now» of the relationship: responding to what is actually occurring rather than to what verbal rules dictate should be occurring.

Something very concrete happens when contact with the present is lost in parenting. Extended temporal frames appear —the catastrophic ALWAYS, the absolutizing NEVER— and they displace the precision of what is happening right now, here, with this specific child today. The regulatory difference is direct: the activation produced by «my child always does this» is qualitatively different from that produced by «my child is doing this now», even if the observable behavior is identical.

✦ Temporal frames that distort

A mother who, when her teenage child arrives home late without notice, slides directly into «this always happens, he never listens to me, this is going to get worse.» The physiological activation produced by that chain of temporal frames is incompatible with the conversation she needs to have. The same situation, when the adult can stay with «he came home late tonight and I need to understand what happened», allows for a response that addresses the fact without collapsing into the pattern.

Parents who describe their eight-year-old as «impossible» or «always the same.» When asked to describe the past week in detail, moments of connection inevitably appear, moments of play, moments when the child cooperated. The extended temporal frame («always») had erased all that information.

Recovering the present moment —this specific moment, this specific child today— is not optimism: it is precision.

Cognitive defusion in the caregiving adult

Caregiving adults operate under the control of complex verbal networks about themselves as parents, about their children, about what «should» happen in parenting, and about the consequences of every decision. When these networks acquire functions of rigid command —when the thought «I am a bad mother» organizes behavior in the same way a direct threat would— the parental repertoire narrows significantly.

Cognitive defusion, in the context of CFP, does not seek for parents to think differently but to establish a different relationship with their thinking: to be able to notice «I'm having the thought that I'm doing this badly» without that thought automatically dictating the next action. This distinction —between the thought as an event that occurs and the thought as an exact description of reality— is what allows you to act from values even when self-critical thoughts are present with force.

This is another important difference from classical cognitive therapy. Cognitive therapy seeks to change the content of thinking: that you think more «rational» or «balanced» things. Defusion does not need the thought to change. It needs the thought to stop commanding. The thought can stay: it doesn't have to disappear for action to change.

Recent research has shown that defusion predicts psychological outcomes independently of changes in the frequency or content of thinking. This has direct implications for psychological work with parents: the goal is not for them to have fewer critical thoughts about their parenting, but for those thoughts to lose their function of controlling behavior in the moment of interaction.

Many parents arrive in session convinced that if they could just stop thinking they are bad parents, everything would go better. CFP proposes something different: the problem is not the thought, but what they do with it.

A parent who thinks «I'm doing this terribly» and yet can sit down to listen to their child, can repair a rupture, and can hold an uncomfortable limit, is exercising psychological flexibility even though the self-critical thought is still present.

In session, this is one of the most important turning points: when the parent stops trying to eliminate that thought and starts observing what happens just after it appears. That is where the real space for work opens up: not on the thought itself, but on what it organizes in behavior when it appears.

Acceptance: the adult who can be with their own distress

Acceptance, as an ACT process, is not resignation or suppression. It is the willingness to make room for difficult emotions —including distress, fear, guilt, or frustration— without acting to eliminate them. In the context of CFP, this is key: often, what we call «protecting the child» is actually a strategy of experiential avoidance, where the adult acts so as not to have to deal with their own distress.

✦ Care that relieves the adult

A father says: «I can't watch him cry like that.» Every time his child gets frustrated, he intervenes quickly: solves, distracts, soothes. From the outside it looks like care. But if you observe with precision, something else is happening: as soon as the crying appears, an urgency also appears in the father. He is not responding to the child. He is responding to what is being activated in him.

The problem is not that he cares too much. It is that he can't hold the distress long enough for the care to truly be for his child and not for relieving himself.

Here we find one of the most frequent —and most difficult to see from inside— patterns in parenting difficulties. When a mother gives in to her child's protest just so they stop crying, the actual function of that behavior may not be the child's well-being. It may be the reduction of the adult's own distress.

There is no intent to harm. It is exactly the opposite: there is so much love that hearing the child's suffering becomes unbearable. But what is relieved is the adult's distress, not the child's need. And with enough repetition, the child learns something no one taught them deliberately: that their distress is an emergency that must be stopped, not an experience that can be moved through.

How can you tell?

One way to tell whether parental behavior is organized by the child's well-being or by avoidance of one's own distress is to ask: would I do this even if it didn't bring me any relief? Would I do it even if no one were watching?

If the honest answer is «probably not», the process organizing that behavior is experiential avoidance. If the answer is «yes, because it is what my child needs now», it is action from values. This is not a judgment. It is information.

✦ In session

A father who cannot watch his nine-year-old cry without going into panic. Every time the child cries, the father does whatever it takes to make it stop: gives him what he was asking for, distracts him, tells him it isn't a big deal. He describes this as «not being able to bear watching my child suffer.» Functionally, what he cannot bear is his own distress in the face of his child's crying.

The child, who needs to learn that they can cry and the world won't end, instead receives the message that their crying is an emergency signal that must be stopped. Over time, they learn not to cry —or to cry strategically when they need to get something.

This pattern intensifies when the adult carries their own history of scarce co-regulation: when as a child there was also no adult who could stay with their distress without shutting it down. Recognizing it is neither an excuse nor a destiny: it is the first step for it to stop operating on autopilot.

Self-as-Context in the adult and in the child

Self-as-Context —the perspective of the observer that persists across time and across the changing content of experience— has, in the context of CFP, a relational function that goes beyond individual use. When an adult can hold the observer's perspective during a conflict with their child, they can simultaneously notice their own activation, stay in contact with the child's perspective, and act from their values of care. Without that perspective, the adult becomes fused with their own experience and loses access to the information needed for a flexible parental response.

✦ Self-as-Context in action

During an argument with her teenage daughter who has come home smelling of tobacco, a mother may collapse into her own indignation («liar, after all we've talked about») and completely lose sight of what her daughter is feeling in that moment: probably fear, shame, and the need to know whether the bond survives what has just been discovered.

The mother who can notice her own indignation —«I notice I'm very angry»— without being captured by it can do both things: set clear limits about the tobacco AND stay in contact with her daughter as a person. The one who is fused with her indignation can only do one of the two.

For the child, the development of Self-as-Context —which occurs progressively across childhood and adolescence— is functionally associated with a history of interactions with adults who consistently maintain that distinction. Episodes of repair after conflicts are particularly relevant: every time the relationship breaks and is repaired without anyone being defined by the mistake, the child experiences that there is a Self that persists across episodes of failure, that is not reduced to what they did wrong or to what they felt in that moment.

Clarification of values and the function of the family bond

Values, in the sense this approach gives them, are not abstract principles or personal preferences: they are chosen directions of action that give meaning and motivation to behavior even when that behavior is costly in the short term. They are not goals to be achieved but directions that are walked every day, in every interaction. To care for, to guide, to protect, and to accompany the child's development are parental values when they organize what you do, not just what you say matters to you.

It is precisely that coherence that functionally distinguishes parenting organized by values from parenting organized by learned rules or by avoidance of distress. A present parent is not the one who is physically in the same space as their child: it is the one who, when with them, has their behavior organized by what the child needs in that moment. A committed parent is not the one who never fails: it is the one who comes back after the failure. A parent who loves unconditionally is not the one who never gets angry: it is the one who, even angry, does not put the child's place in the relationship in question. These values are what allow the adult to hold limits that produce immediate distress —in the child and in themselves— because the horizon they provide is wider than the relief of the moment. The security the child needs is not the absence of conflict: it is the certainty, installed by the history of what they have lived through in the relationship, that the adult in front of them is acting from something more stable than the day's emotional state.

The distinction between value and learned rule is critical in psychological work with parents. A parent who avoids setting limits «because they want their child to be happy» may be acting from a genuine value (the child's well-being) or from a learned rule («good parents do not cause distress in their children») whose actual function is to reduce their own guilt. The functional distinction —would this behavior keep occurring if no one saw it? would it still matter if there were no social consequences?— allows us to identify the process that really organizes parental behavior in that moment.

Key question

What kind of adult do you want your child to be twenty years from now? When parents answer that question —«able to face difficulties», «able to ask for help», «honest even when it's uncomfortable»— and then ask themselves whether what they are doing today contributes to that, a visible gap often appears.

✦ Value vs. learned rule

A father says he wants his child to be able to tolerate frustration. That is his intention. But when his child starts to get frustrated, he intervenes before the distress grows: he helps more than necessary, changes the situation, or gives in. Not because he doesn't believe in that value, but because in that moment he can't hold what the moment demands.

The father who wants his child to tolerate frustration but who systematically protects them from any frustrating experience is acting from the rule («I can't see my child upset») rather than from the value. Naming that gap is not judging the father: it is offering him information about the process organizing his behavior.

Committed action under high activation

Committed action —acting in the direction of values even when internal experience pushes in another direction— is the process most frequently misinterpreted in its application to parenting. The most common confusion is to equate committed action with action without distress: the parent who sets a limit «from a place of calm.» But committed action under real parenting conditions frequently occurs in the presence of intense activation, tiredness, frustration, or guilt.

What distinguishes committed action from impulsive reaction is not the absence of distress but the adult's relationship with that distress: whether behavior is organized by the impulse to eliminate distress (experiential avoidance) or by the direction values provide (even with distress present). This distinction is functionally precise and psychologically observable: it produces different patterns of behavior, different relational consequences, and, over time, different developmental histories for the child.

✦ Committed action under pressure

A father may have an exhausting day at work, get home, and find his twelve-year-old who has ignored their agreement on phone use for the third time. The activation he feels is real. Committed action does not consist of not feeling it: it consists of not letting that activation dictate whether or not the agreed-upon consequence is applied.

The child receives the message that agreements hold even when the adult is tired; and they also receive, implicitly, the model of an adult who acts from what matters to them even when the situation is hard.

The caregiving adult's self-compassion: a differentiated process

Self-compassion is not heightened acceptance or elevated emotional sensitivity. It is a functionally specific process that CFP identifies as a necessary condition for the other processes to be sustainable under the real conditions of parenting.

Acceptance, as described above, is the willingness to contact internal experience without acting to eliminate it. An adult can practice acceptance from neutrality, from coldness, and even from contempt toward their own experience: «I accept that I'm exhausted, that I'm doing this badly, that I'm probably failing my child.» That sentence contains acceptance —no flight from the content— but no relational quality to sustain it over the long term.

Self-compassion adds to the process of acceptance the same quality of attention the adult can offer the child in a moment of distress: recognition of the experience as real, recognition of imperfection as part of the shared human condition, and a response from care rather than from judgment.

This distinction has direct functional consequences. An adult who operates under severe self-criticism and high demand toward their own mistakes tends to reproduce, in the relationship with the child, the same evaluative structure: the child's mistake takes on threat functions equivalent to those the adult's own mistake has for them. The mother who cannot make a mistake without collapsing into guilt will have enormous difficulty being calm when her child makes theirs. Not because she doesn't want to. But because her nervous system already has it installed that mistakes are dangerous, that they deserve condemnation, that they must be eliminated as soon as possible. And that signal reaches the child without words.

Self-compassion is not telling yourself everything is fine when it isn't. It is being able to say to yourself: «I made a mistake, what I did was clumsy, and that doesn't define me». And then to keep going. That sequence is exactly the one the child needs to see modeled in order to apply it in their own life.

If you have spent a long time reading about parenting from guilt, you have probably been trying to improve as a mother or father from the very place from which it is hardest to do so: demand without compassion. The same work this text proposes for the relationship with the child —to accept experience without fleeing, to distinguish behavior from identity, to act from values even with distress present— is applicable, first of all, to the relationship with yourself.

Evidence Parental self-compassion: what the research says

Recent research on parental psychological flexibility confirms that the adult's self-compassion independently predicts the quality of the parental bond, with greater strength than general emotional sensitivity or knowledge of child development.

A parent who can say «I made a mistake and what I did was clumsy, and it doesn't define me» has access to a regulation of the nervous system that the parent oscillating between justification and self-condemnation cannot sustain. This regulation is the physiological and psychological condition for presence, validation, and repair to be not deliberate efforts but accessible repertoires.

✦ The guilt that paralyzes repair

A mother, after an exhausting day, shouts at her nine-year-old over something minor, realizes it, and for the next two hours becomes trapped in the thought «I'm a horrible mother.» She does not initiate the repair because the guilt paralyzes her. Her child goes to sleep without anyone repairing what happened.

Self-compassion could have produced another sequence: «I went too far, I'm very tired, that doesn't justify what I did but it also doesn't make me a bad mother —I'm going to fix it.»
