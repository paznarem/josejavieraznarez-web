The three stages: from closeness to autonomy

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 12 min read

← Back to blog

This is the second article in the series Co-sleeping — Parenting with direction. In the previous article we explained what conscious co-sleeping is from the standpoint of contextual psychology and why it makes sense. Here we walk through what happens in each stage of the first three years.

No two families are alike, and no two babies are alike. But there is a journey that, with variations, repeats itself: the child who at first needs the caregiver's body to calm down gradually gains the capacity to calm down with less. Not because they are abandoned, but because what they have lived night after night has been giving them resources. That journey has three recognisable stages.

0–6 months: closeness as essential care

These first months are exhausting in a way few words capture well. You are not failing if you are exhausted. You are not failing if sometimes you don't know whether you're doing it well. What you are doing — being there, responding, holding — is exactly what your baby needs, even when it doesn't feel that way to you.

During the first six months of life, co-sleeping is a natural extension of maternal care. The newborn depends on caregivers for everything, from feeding to body-temperature regulation. In this context, co-sleeping serves concrete functions.

It facilitates night-time breastfeeding, reducing the baby's stress and supporting better rest for the mother.

It promotes physiological regulation: when a newborn sleeps next to the caregiver's body, their temperature stabilises, their breathing regulates, their heart rate syncs with the person beside them. The adult body literally acts as a regulating environment for the baby's body — before language exists, before anything can be explained. And this direct physiological regulation does not disappear when the baby grows and language emerges: it coexists with it, and when something disturbs it, intervention is needed at that same bodily level, not only at the symbolic one.

It strengthens the bond, providing the baby with an environment of protection and emotional stability. The history of consistent caregiver responses to each of the baby's signals — what in the previous article we described as contingencies of reinforcement — generates the first patterns of relational behavior. It is here that those frames of coordination between «caregiver» and «safety» begin to form: the first nucleus of what will later allow the child to derive that safety symbolically, in the caregiver's physical absence.

6–18 months: balancing closeness and autonomy

As the baby grows and becomes more mobile, the dynamics of co-sleeping change. The child is in an intense process of cognitive and emotional development, and many babies experience separation anxiety at night, which leads them to wake more often seeking the comfort of their caregivers. Co-sleeping helps mitigate these awakenings, providing a steady sense of safety.

At some point in this stage — hard to pinpoint precisely because it happens gradually — something changes: the baby starts to calm down not only because the caregiver is physically present, but because they have learned that the caregiver comes back. That apparently small difference is in fact enormous. It means the child has begun to distinguish between now and later, between present separation and future reunion. This is exactly what Relational Frame Theory (RFT) describes as temporal deictic frames — the distinctions between I and you, here and there, now and then introduced in the previous article — applied to time. The caregiver's work in this phase consists of reinforcing that capacity through words that sustain it — «I will be here when you need me» — moving the baby's emotion regulation gradually from the purely bodily to the verbal.

Window of tolerance

The challenge in this phase is balancing the child's need for closeness with the caregivers' sleep quality. Conscious co-sleeping allows for closeness when the baby needs it and offers small opportunities for distancing. These opportunities are also moments of exposure — making gradual contact with what is avoided — practised with psychological flexibility: the capacity to maintain or change what we do in line with what matters to us, even when difficult emotions show up. The caregiver models for the child the capacity to tolerate distress without it being a signal of threat, expanding what is called the window of tolerance: the range of emotional activation within which one can keep thinking, feeling, and acting clearly. Some caregivers choose to begin transitioning from co-sleeping in the same bed to a cot in the same room, a natural first step toward more autonomous sleep regulation.

18–36 months: progressive independence

From eighteen months on, children develop greater autonomy both during the day and at night. The desire for physical closeness can intensify with changes such as weaning or starting nursery. Children seek co-sleeping to deal with these changes: caregiver availability at night gives them a stable context for processing new experiences, reinforcing their flexible regulatory capacity while moving toward greater independence in sleep.

There is no universal timetable for this transition. Each family has its own rhythm, and that rhythm emerges from the particular history of each bond: how the caregiver has responded, how the child has been developing their capacity to regulate, what changes have happened in family life. When the transition stalls or generates sustained distress that does not ease, it can be useful to have the support of a contextual psychologist who helps identify what is maintaining the difficulty in this specific case — not in general, but in this family, with this child, at this moment.

Technical note Process-Based Therapy (PBT): what to assess when the transition stalls

A process-based assessment — what in this approach is called Process-Based Therapy (PBT): intervening on the specific psychological processes maintaining the problem in this person and at this moment, rather than applying a general protocol based on diagnosis — allows us to identify what is sustaining the difficulty. Is the child seeking night-time closeness because immediate relief from distress is what maintains that behavior? Because they have not yet developed enough self-regulation resources? Because something in the caregiver's response makes it harder for what is learned in one context to transfer to new contexts? Identifying which process is at work allows us to design an intervention tailored to that family.

From age two onwards, many families begin transitioning toward a separate room, gradually and tailored to the needs of each child: the «transition bed,» partial co-sleeping in the same room but in separate beds.

The sign of a well-supported transition

A well-supported transition at this stage has a recognisable sign: the child can stay in their bed, in their room, without separation being lived as a threat. Not because they have learned to suppress the need for connection, but because they have built, night after night, the certainty that the caregiver exists even when out of sight. Everything that has been done before — responding consistently, naming emotions, tolerating distress without erasing it — has been building exactly that: the capacity to carry the caregiver inside, without needing to have them right next to you.

In more technical terms: the child has developed the capacity to feel safe and accompanied thanks to the network of shared experiences during that history of shared nights, without needing physical contact. And something more: their sense of self — what in ACT is called self-as-context, the perspective from which everything happens — begins to organise itself as an inner place that can hold difficult experiences, including those of distress, without those experiences defining who they are or putting their safety in question.

Bond and functional history

There are caregivers who stop co-sleeping and wonder if they did it too soon or too late. There are others who keep it longer than they had planned and wonder if they have created a dependency. That worry has an implicit assumption worth examining: that the bond is built or damaged through one-off decisions. From Contextual Behavioral Science (CBS), it is not so. The development of the bond cannot be understood through isolated moments, but only in its functional context: what shapes the child is not any specific decision, but the patterns that repeat over time.

The unit of analysis in this approach is always the act-in-context: not isolated behavior nor an internal quality of the child, but what the child does in relation to what is happening around them. From very early on, the child learns to connect — through those networks of relational frames — caregiver presence with experiences of safety or threat, configuring patterns that will guide their behavior throughout life.

It is not any specific decision that builds or breaks your child. It is the overall direction of your presence. And if you are here, that direction already exists.

What is usually described as the capacity to «detach with safety» is the expression of a flexible repertoire — the set of responses a person has available — built on a history of consistent contingencies. The child can explore the world because separation has stopped functioning as a threat: not because the discomfort of separating has been removed, but because the child has learned that the caregiver remains available even when not in sight. That trust acts as a context from which the child can make room for what they feel — what in ACT is called acceptance: not resigning, but making space for inner experience without spending energy trying to remove it — without needing control behaviors that take them away from what matters.

Real deprivation of affection, both as physical and emotional contact, has lasting impact. A child without access to an emotionally balanced environment — without love, without presence, and without safety, including also displeasure — can develop patterns of experiential avoidance — the tendency to organise life around not feeling what hurts — that will limit their capacity to face difficult experiences in the future, blocking the process of psychological flexibility before it has the chance to consolidate.

If at any point in these pages you've felt that this is too much, pause for a moment here. The fact that it matters to you so much — that you are searching, reading, wondering — already says something very specific about who you are as a mother. Not a perfect mother. Something more useful than that: a mother who has not stopped caring.
