Parenting with direction

The key to healthy parenting: the compassionate balance between giving and receiving

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 9 min read

Scene

A daughter comes home. She doesn't say hello. She walks past her mother and shuts herself in her room.

The mother hesitates for a few seconds. She feels a knot in her chest. She thinks: "If I don't say anything to her, she'll think I don't care."

She goes upstairs. Knocks. Asks. Insists.

The daughter answers from inside: "Leave me alone. It's always the same. All you know how to do is control."

Neither of them yet knows that they are not fighting against each other. Neither of them is trying to cause harm. Each, in her own way, is fighting against the fear that gets activated inside the bond. And yet, the bond deteriorates in that very moment.

The true legacy of parents should not be silent sacrifice, but the active teaching of an ethic of responsible mutual care, where giving and receiving are fundamental principles of family life.
— José Javier

The model of parenting based on unconditional sacrifice and protection without real practice of mutual care can contribute, in interaction with many other variables, to family relationships that grow increasingly fragile. This does not happen all at once; it is not a visible rupture. It happens in small repeated moments: when one gives without limit and the other receives without practicing how to give back. What breaks is not love, but the direction of the bond.

When that reciprocity is not consistently practiced during childhood, the consequences appear later at every stage: in adolescence, as demand without structure; in adulthood, as reproach; in later stages, as difficulty in caring for one another.

These patterns do not emerge from a single variable, nor can they be explained exclusively by parenting history. The behavior of children — especially in adolescence — is influenced at the same time by many different factors, in multiple simultaneous contexts: peer group, digital environment, cultural models, individual characteristics and accumulated verbal learning. The family is a key context, but not the only one.

That wear and tear does not belong to a single family or a single era. The tension between care, authority, gratitude and reciprocity runs through the history of relationships between generations.

A mirror that comes from far back

We sometimes think this knot in the stomach is a 21st-century invention, but humanity has spent millennia trying to solve the same equation: how do we care for one another without smothering each other?

Looking back, the famous "Honor your father and your mother" was not born as a punishment, but as the first "life insurance policy" in history. In Egypt it was called Ma'at; in Rome, Pietas; in indigenous cultures, it was respect for the wise. It was not blind obedience, but pure functionality: reciprocity was the glue that kept the tribe from falling apart.

From Judaism to Buddhism, including Confucianism, every culture has insisted on the same thing for a practical reason: the relationship of mutual care is the only one that sustains the group over the long term. It is not an abstract value; it is what allows the cycle not to break when parents grow old and children grow up.

Something changed in the West, however. We moved from that historical "glue" to a new model: the model of total sacrifice.

The Western transformation: from honor to limitless sacrifice

Starting in the last third of the 20th century, the relationship between parents and children underwent a radical shift. We moved from a model based on authority and respect to one centered almost exclusively on the child's emotional well-being.

Up until the 1950s, the family was a hierarchy: parents ruled and children obeyed. It was a rigid system, sometimes excessively so, but the roles were clear. With the social revolutions of the 60s and 70s, however, we questioned all authority, including parental authority. We wanted families that were more democratic, more affectionate, more horizontal. And that was a great step forward… but it brought an invisible trap.

In trying to be the "perfect" parents who validate every emotion of their children, many fell into the mistake of erasing their own limits. Television, social media and thousands of parenting books reinforced a dangerous idea: that true love is absolute, unconditional sacrifice.

The idea began to be rewarded that parents must give themselves to the point of exhaustion, giving up their health or their identity, without asking for anything in return. But this is not a question of generosity, it is pure logic: when a relationship stops working in two directions, it begins to wear thin, even though the affection is still there.

Parenting without reciprocity: the learning of automatic entitlement

By removing any expectation of return, we are not making life easier for our children; we are teaching them a false rule about the real world.

If a teenager grows up seeing that their wishes are met consistently and without any condition of reciprocity, they do not become "bad", they simply learn that they have an automatic right to receive. They learn that their well-being occupies the center of the relational space, and that the responsibility for them being well always belongs to someone else.

It is a silent learning that, years later, becomes an insurmountable wall within the family itself: a pattern in which demanding has been reinforced — that is, has worked so many times that it repeats almost on its own — while contributing has never been required.

The final paradox is that many parents who sacrificed their personal well-being for their children later find themselves with children who do not perceive that care as a reference for connection, but as an unwanted imposition, a source of distress, or something later experienced in traumatic terms.

Many parents recognize this moment with a very specific feeling: "I have been there for everything… and now it seems it doesn't count." Not because their children lack values, but because they have not had enough opportunities to practice them within the bond.

A parent picks up, fixes, pays, organizes, remembers, anticipates and cushions. They do it out of love, out of protection, to make life easier in the present and the future. But, without realizing it, they are clearing from the path almost every occasion in which their child could wait, get frustrated, cooperate, support, comfort, repair or care for someone else. Years later, that same parent wonders why their child seems unable to perceive and attend to others' needs. The answer is not always lack of love. Sometimes it is lack of practice.

We are not saying the child is selfish by nature. It is not something they "are", like having brown eyes. We are talking about a learned pattern: a way of relating that has been consolidating little by little, without anyone setting out to do so. A way in which, without anyone explicitly saying it, the child has learned that they can receive affection, attention and resources without having to give anything in return.

Conceptual framework

Contextual Behavioral Science — CBS: an approach within scientific psychology that always studies human behavior in relation to the context in which it occurs — explains it like this: what has happened here is not that the child "is" a certain way. It is that certain responses have been consistently reinforced and others have not had the opportunity to develop.

The result is a limited repertoire — that is, the set of responses that this person actually has available to relate to others — in which demanding has been consistently reinforced and contributing has not been required, which displaces the learning of values such as kindness, gratitude and mutual care.

This pattern does not imply intent or lack of values. It implies a specific learning history.

The emphasis on receiving rather than giving

The culture of immediate gratification, intensified by technological advances and the overabundance of goods and services, reinforces this pattern: the consequences of one's own actions arrive immediately and without prior effort. What is not practiced is giving and contributing. And what is not practiced under the everyday conditions of the family bond will not be available when the bond requires it.

When this pattern repeats over years, it stops affecting only specific behaviors and begins to reorganize the position each member occupies within the family bond. That is where the difficulty no longer is limited to receiving more than one gives, but alters the relational structure itself.

In the current context, this process is amplified by digital environments where validation is immediate, visible and constant. The teenager compares their family experience with external models that present certain forms of relationship as the standard, which can intensify the perception of injustice or insufficiency in the bond with their parents.

The confusion of roles

In families where parents are focused on satisfying their children's wishes without setting clear limits, a functional inversion of roles can occur. The children, instead of being guided, begin to organize family decisions. The parents enter a cycle of compliance to avoid short-term conflict. The children, in turn, consolidate a way of operating in which their demand produces an effect without needing to consider its impact on others. And what is barely practiced in all of this is truly putting yourself in the other person's place: compassionate empathy — not just understanding what the other feels, but having that matter to you and move you to act.

In some cases, this process evolves into a form of distancing especially common today: silent cancellation. The teenager does not openly confront, but withdraws emotional access, limits the information they share, reduces interaction to the bare minimum, and reorganizes their relational life outside the family system.

It does not always appear, nor does it correspond to a single learning history, but it can become consolidated when the teenager finds in relational withdrawal an effective way to reduce distress, protect their image, or recover a sense of control.

This pattern of distancing is not simply rebellion or rejection. It has an internal logic with clear functions: pulling away is the way the teenager has found to protect themselves from what they experience as criticism, control, or the feeling of not being enough, in contexts that are experienced as evaluative or invalidating.

When the absence of reciprocity begins to organize the story of harm

In many cases, children develop behaviors of appealing to their own distress — crying, reproach, the expression of suffering — which, in their learning history, have been effective in getting what they wanted, regardless of the long-term relational cost. And so they repeat them, not necessarily in a conscious or manipulative way.

At this point it is key to introduce another variable: the teenager does not respond only to what happens, but to how they interpret it. Here what contextual psychology calls verbal rules comes into play: phrases the person has constructed from their experience, without anyone having explicitly told them, and which organize what they do. "My parents don't respect me." "They control me." "It's never enough." These are not just stray ideas. They are rules that function as automatic instructions: when they appear, they direct behavior almost without the person deciding.

What you have just read does not happen only in the teenager's head. It happens in the relationship, in real time.

JJ: Contextual psychologist; H: daughter; M: mother.

H— Don't ask me anything else. It's always the same.

M— I just need to know where you are, that's normal. You can't just disappear like that.

H— See? Control. Always control. All you know how to do is control.

M— It's not control, it's worry. If I don't say anything, then it looks like I don't care.

H— I don't care how it looks. You don't understand anything. You never care how I feel.

The scene does not begin with a lack of love. It begins with two responses that work in the short term. The daughter pulls away and reduces what she feels. The mother insists and reduces her own uncertainty. And both, without knowing it, reinforce exactly what hurts them.

JJ— Stop.

He doesn't explain. He doesn't correct. He interrupts the pattern.

JJ— Don't explain anything to me. Just notice what is happening right now, here.

(He looks at the mother)

JJ— What is in your body right now?

M— …tiredness. And something in my chest.

JJ— Stay with that.

(He looks at the daughter)

JJ— And you?

H— Strange.

JJ— Strange uncomfortable, or strange different?

H— …different.

For the first time, they are not arguing. They are noticing.

JJ— When you say "control"… are you noticing it, or are you believing it?

H— But it's true.

JJ— Right now your mind is not letting you see it any other way. And when it shows up… what does it ask you to do?

H— Walk past. Say nothing.

JJ— Shut down. And you, when she shuts down… what shows up?

M— That I'm losing her.

JJ— And what do you do?

M— Insist more.

Here is the loop. It is not theoretical. It is visible. The more "she doesn't care about me" shows up, the more she pulls away. The more "I'm losing her" shows up, the more the mother insists.

JJ— Look at this. (To the mother) Place a hand on your chest.

It's not a technique. It is making the function visible.

JJ— (To the daughter) Look at that hand. What is it trying to protect?

H— …her fear.

JJ— Exactly. (To the mother) And when she goes quiet… what is she protecting?

M— …her freedom.

At this point something key happens. Behavior does not change yet. What each of them sees in the other does.

JJ— You don't want opposite things. You want important things. But you are defending them in a way that breaks them.

JJ— Your mind tells you: "If I don't ask, I'm a bad mother." And when it shows up… you obey.

JJ— And your mind tells you: "If she controls me, she doesn't respect me." And when it shows up… you leave.

This is what in many homes is experienced as character, rebellion or disrespect. But functionally it is something else: responses that have been gradually learned and that activate almost automatically in certain situations, under certain conditions, without us deciding consciously. It is what in this approach is called a response pattern: not a conscious decision each time, but a learned reaction that is triggered under certain conditions.

JJ— Look at her. (To the daughter) What happens to her when she doesn't hear from you?

H— …fear.

JJ— That fear is not going to go away. Because it comes from something important.

Here the limit that is often avoided being named appears.

JJ— Your mother is responsible for your safety. And that is not negotiable. But you can change how you respond to that.

JJ— What do you need in this relationship?

H— …air.

JJ— Space.

M— Not to lose her.

JJ— Connection. Autonomy and bond are not opposites. They break when one tries to hold them up alone.

(Silence)

JJ— Now comes the hard part. Not understanding. Doing.

M— …waiting before asking.

H— …saying something and not disappearing.

They are not big changes. They are small movements under intense emotions. That is where reciprocity is learned. The scene does not end with an agreement. It ends with something more important: two people seeing for the first time what they are doing… while they are doing it.

This kind of interaction is not an exception. It is representative of a functional pattern that, when repeated, organizes the bond over time.

When a verbal rule like "they control me" becomes consolidated, it stops functioning only as an idea and begins to organize the perception of the bond. Everything the parent does passes through that lens, and what does not fit it tends to stop being seen.

These rules, once established, organize future behavior: they filter what is heard, narrow what the child is willing to show, increase the likelihood of concealment, confrontation or withdrawal, and can be maintained even when current conditions have changed.

The result of this dynamic is that many children build a reading of human relationships in which their wishes and needs are perceived as a priority and the responsibility for fulfilling them always falls on someone else.

The impact across different developmental stages

Imbalanced parenting generates a cycle with functional consequences in three critical stages: adolescence, the children's adulthood, and the parents' old age.

Adolescence: demand without structure

The teenager is still building their repertoire — the set of responses they have available to navigate life — especially in what has to do with regulating distress and acting with what comes next in mind, not just with immediate relief.

In contexts where immediate satisfaction has been prioritized without requiring reciprocity, verbal rules of the type "I have a right to receive without needing to commit to anything" become consolidated. Their right is not conditional on any duty. The absence of coherent limits produces teenagers with low frustration tolerance and a pattern of automatic demand.

Many of the behaviors parents describe as rebellion — hiding, lying, responding with hostility or withdrawing — can be understood functionally as attempts to regulate immediate distress. They are not solely behaviors against the parents, but behaviors aimed at reducing the perceived sense of evaluation, control or insufficiency.

Many teenagers do not feel that they are damaging their bond with their parents. They feel they are trying to breathe inside it. The problem is that, when breathing only seems possible by pulling away, hiding or attacking, the relief of one moment can become the wound of many months.

Even with norms based on solid values, they can defy authority figures, who love and protect them, as a way of testing limits and gaining control, without their repertoire including consideration of the emotional consequences for others.

Adulthood: narratives of victimization and resentment

This analysis does not imply that parents have not been present, committed or available. Many parents in these situations have sustained the bond for years, have cared, protected and offered resources consistently. However, that does not guarantee that the child interprets that bond in the same way.

Parenting without reciprocity organizes the way the individual reads their own story and their place in relationships. The transition into adulthood implies taking responsibility for one's own life. However, when development has been marked by avoidance of frustration and externalization of problems, dynamics emerge in which the individual systematically perceives themselves as the result of what others did or failed to do.

Blaming parents for perceived shortcomings. Even when parents have provided love and resources in abundance, the absence of practice in reciprocity generates a verbal rule of the type "what I received was not enough", which organizes present behavior independently of what actually happened.

There are phrases that, once repeated many times inside, accompanied by voices that validate them, stop describing the story and start organizing it. "It wasn't enough", "they never understood me", "they always demanded too much", "they controlled me". Sometimes they contain part of the truth. But they can also become rigid frames that no longer allow seeing nuances, effort, ambivalence, or one's own responsibility.

Prolonged repetition of dependency patterns. Having not developed autonomy as a response practiced in context, many adults seek in their relationships a substitute for the parental role, expecting to be cared for without taking on equitable responsibilities.

So the memory of their childhood and adolescent history is transformed into a narrative of victimization that fulfills a specific function: it relieves distress in the short term, because if the blame is always with others, there is nothing I have to change. But in the long term it narrows the available repertoire and pulls the individual away from the contexts where they could learn that their actions have consequences and that those consequences can change.

Framework What makes contextual psychology distinctive here?

This is where the contextual psychology view differs from other approaches. It does not say: "that thought is distorted, change it" — that would be a cognitive approach. It does not say: "the cause is in an unconscious conflict from your childhood" — that would be a dynamic approach.

It says: that narrative has a function in your life now. What are you getting out of it? And what is it costing you? The question is not whether the narrative is true or false, but what effect it has on what you do and on the direction your life is taking.

Parents' old age: abandonment and conflicts among siblings

When parents can no longer sustain the role of unconditional providers, they meet children who, far from incorporating reciprocity as a learned relational pattern, prioritize their personal well-being and neglect the family bond. Children who have grown up in a context where receiving did not require giving may read the care of aging parents as an "uncorresponded obligation" and resist taking on that responsibility, precisely because that movement — giving without receiving immediately in return — is not part of their practiced repertoire.

Siblings can come into conflict over the distribution of responsibilities, deepening family fragmentation. Children may sustain the verbal rule that their parents "already lived their life" and that now it is their turn to "enjoy theirs" without any added burden.

Toward conscious and compassionate parenting

Breaking this cycle does not require a prescribed parenting model or a single way of organizing the family. It requires identifying which conditions produce which consequences, and making decisions based on the values — in the sense of chosen directions that give meaning to what you do, not mere preferences — of each father and mother, about the kind of bond they want to build and pass on.

What research in contextual behavioral science shows is that response patterns become consolidated through repeated practice under everyday conditions, not through grand statements of intent. A child learns reciprocity when reciprocity is consistently required in the ordinary family context: when contributing is a condition of the bond, when frustration is not removed immediately, when limits are not a threat but an expectable part of living together.

Principle

Reciprocity is not transmitted as a moral obligation, but as a functional experience: the child learns to contribute when contributing has real effects within the bond.

For that to happen, parents need to be able to distinguish between two movements that are sometimes confused: protecting and depriving of the opportunity to learn. Giving is an essential part of parenting. But giving without ever requiring the reciprocity that the child or teenager can already offer is not generosity: it is the elimination of the contexts where one learns that the bond works in both directions.

The true legacy of parents is not silent sacrifice, but the active teaching — practiced, repeated, embodied in ordinary moments — of an ethic of responsible mutual care, where giving and receiving are fundamental principles of family life.

This is not transmitted with words. It is transmitted in how the bond functions day after day. And when that functioning includes reciprocity from early on, the child not only understands it: they practice it, incorporate it into their way of being with others, and carry it with them as they build their own relationships.

Perhaps the question is not only how much you have given for your child, but also whether you have left them enough opportunities to learn how to give. And perhaps the question for the child is not only how much they have received, but at what point they began to consider it normal to receive without looking at the cost that this had for others.
