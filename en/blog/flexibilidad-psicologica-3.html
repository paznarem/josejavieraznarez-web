When you know it isn't dangerous… yet still can't do it

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 10 min read

← Back to blog

There are very small situations that can become enormous. Sending a message. Speaking in front of others. Saying something out loud. Making plans with someone.

Before doing them, something shows up inside: tension, racing thoughts, an unease that's hard to explain. And at the same time, a mental clarity: "it's not that big a deal," "it shouldn't be this hard," "there's no reason to feel like this."

And even so, you don't do it. Or you do it with great effort, already bracing for the end. Or you simply avoid it. And little by little, without noticing, your life starts organising itself around that.

What you feel makes sense, even if it doesn't seem to

Before continuing, there's something that needs saying. Feeling this way for a long time has a cost. Not just the cost of the situations you avoid. Also the cost of explaining to yourself why something happens to you that other people seem to do without effort. Of trying, not managing, and drawing conclusions about what that says about you. That is exhausting. And it makes sense that it is.

What you feel is not a sign that something is broken in you. It's a sign that, with your experience, your organism learned to respond in a way that, at some point, had a logic, a real function: to protect you from something that was then genuinely threatening, or at least experienced as such.

The problem isn't that that learning happened. The problem is that it generalised: it now activates in situations that don't need it, with the same intensity as before. You're not too sensitive. You're someone whose organism learned a lesson too well — one that no longer applies.

That doesn't change by telling yourself you shouldn't feel it. But it can change if you start relating to it differently.

It isn't a problem of understanding it better

It isn't that you don't understand it. You can reason perfectly well that there is no real danger. You can explain it. You can see it clearly. And yet, in the moment, you do something else.

This often leads to a hard conclusion: "Then something must be wrong with me." But if you observe what happens more closely, you'll see something else: you aren't failing. You're responding to how your experience is functioning in that moment. And that doesn't change with more arguments.

The point where trying to control it stops helping

You've probably already tried this: calming yourself before speaking, convincing yourself it's fine, waiting until you feel ready, avoiding until "it goes away." And sometimes it works. In the short term.

But if you look at the whole picture, a necessary question appears: is that bringing you closer to the life you want to live? Because the more you need to feel calm before acting, the less you act. And the more you avoid what activates you, the heavier it gets next time.

The key recognition

Continuing to try to control this before acting keeps you stuck in the same waiting. That recognition isn't giving up. It's stopping insisting on a solution that isn't working.

How the loop organises itself

In these situations something very fast happens:

Anxiety appears → thoughts appear ("don't do it," "you're going to look bad," "get out of here") → urgency to leave appears → you avoid, withdraw, postpone → there is relief → the cycle repeats.

You don't need to fully believe those thoughts. It's enough for them to function as a signal. And the relief that follows reinforces exactly that pattern. Not because it's what you want, but because it reduces what you feel.

What matters to recognise

This loop says nothing about your character. It says something about how behavioural patterns are maintained: through the immediate relief that avoidance produces. Anyone, in the right circumstances, can get trapped in a loop like this. And many people are — in different areas of their lives — without knowing it.

Recognising it doesn't resolve it, but it's the only point from which anything can change.

Introducing a minimal difference

When "I can't do this" shows up, you can do something very small: "Here and now, I am having the thought that I can't do this." This doesn't change what you feel. It changes how it influences what you do. That thought stops being an automatic command. And a small margin appears.

If you pause for a second right at that moment — before avoiding, before withdrawing — you can notice something more: anxiety rises and falls, thoughts change, the urgency to leave comes and goes. But there is something present while all of that happens: the place from which you are becoming aware that it's happening.

From there you can notice: "Right now there is tension." "In this moment there is a pull not to do it." "Right now, this is very uncomfortable." Without becoming that. And from that place something happens that changes everything: you can choose where to move, even if what you feel hasn't changed at all.

The question that opens movement

In that moment, the question changes. It's no longer "how do I stop feeling this?" — because that sends you back into the same loop. The one that opens something different is: "If this weren't deciding for me, what would I do now?" Not perfectly. Not completely. Just the next thing.

For that question to have an answer, you don't need great clarity. Is there something in this situation that connects with how you want to relate to others? With something you value about yourself, even if you can't show it right now? With not letting fear end up deciding for you who you are? That's already enough direction to take a step.

Acting when it isn't easy

There's a very widespread rule: "I have to feel safe before I can do it." But in practice it works the other way round: when you do something in that direction, even when it costs, is when something starts to change. Not before. That's why the move isn't to wait for the anxiety to drop. It's to do something with it still present.

It's worth clarifying what that means: it's not gritting your teeth until the distress passes on its own. It's not an endurance test. It's doing something that matters — speaking, staying, responding — while anxiety is still there, without that anxiety being the one deciding whether you do it or not.

What changes over time isn't that anxiety disappears before you act. It's that you show, again and again, that you can move even when it's present. And that transforms your relationship with it: not because you've eliminated it, but because you've acted despite it, in the direction of something that matters to you.

Not everything that makes you feel better brings you closer

There are decisions that reduce discomfort, and decisions that expand your life. Sometimes they coincide, but often they don't. Avoiding can relieve you, but it can also shrink your world. Moving towards something can be uncomfortable, but it can also open it up.

If the criterion is feeling better, your behaviour will be limited. If the criterion is moving forward in what matters, your behaviour can expand, even if it isn't always pleasant.

How you treat yourself when you don't manage it

There's a particularly delicate moment: when you don't manage it. When you avoid, when you leave, when you don't do what you wanted to. And then: "Again." "You're no good at this." "You should be able to."

That doesn't help. It adds more pressure, more threat, less room. The different move here isn't to demand less of yourself. It's not to add more weight to something that's already costly: "This is being hard." "It makes sense that it costs." "I can try again." And from there, being able to continue.

What starts to change

When these moves are repeated, even imperfectly, you avoid less, you stay more, you participate more. Your world expands. Anxiety may still show up with similar intensity in similar situations. What changes isn't that it disappears. It's what happens in you when it's present. And that, over time, is enough for your life to open up.

You don't need to eliminate it. You need something more precise: not to let it decide for you.

Closing the trilogy

Across these three texts on psychological flexibility there is a common thread. When everything goes quiet and it's hard to get started. When everything weighs and life narrows. When everything activates and anticipation takes over. In all three cases, the pattern is the same: trying to feel good before living ends up limiting your life.

And the alternative is also the same in all three: not organising your life around controlling what you feel, but around what makes it worth living.

It is built like this, again and again: notice · let be · return · choose · act

Not as perfection. As practice. It isn't easier. But it is broader, more flexible, and more real.

And from there, even if not everything is in order, even if not everything is comfortable, even if not everything is resolved, your life stops shrinking. And starts, once again, to open.

Three texts, one person

You may have recognised things from all three as you read. That sometimes what predominates is the tiredness and life narrowing. Other times, the activation and anticipation. And other times, simply the feeling that something is holding you back even if you can't quite explain what.

That's to be expected. These aren't three different types of people. They're three ways the same pattern can show up — at different moments, or even mixed together — in the same person. The work isn't to identify which one you fit into definitively. It's to recognise when it's happening, whatever form it takes, and to be able to choose from there.

On the sequence

One last thing about the sequence that appears at the end of each text — notice, let be, return, choose, act: it isn't a recipe with steps in order. Sometimes you act before you've finished noticing. Sometimes you come back to the present moment after avoiding for days. Sometimes letting be arrives after you thought you'd already failed.

What matters isn't following it in that order. It's that those moves keep appearing, imperfectly and repeatedly, in the direction of what matters to you.

The real work

Reading about this has a real limit, and it's worth naming it directly. You can recognise yourself in every paragraph, understand the mechanism, see the pattern clearly. And the moment anxiety shows up, or the tiredness, or the urgency to avoid, everything you've read may fall away. Not because you didn't understand it. But because understanding isn't enough to change the function of the experience.

That work happens in practice, repeated, and is generally done better when accompanied. Not because you can't do it alone. But because there are things that can only be seen from the outside, and there are moves that hold better when someone is there to see them with you.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy isn't a set of techniques for feeling better. It's a learning process that happens in relationship, with someone who can see what you cannot see from the inside.

If something in this trilogy resonates with your experience, that is the moment to take the step. Not when you feel ready. Now, from where you are. Not to resolve this before living. But to learn to live while resolving it.
