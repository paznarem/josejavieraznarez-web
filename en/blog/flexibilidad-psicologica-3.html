When you know it isn't dangerous… yet still can't do it

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 10 min read

← Back to blog

There are very small situations that can become enormous. Sending a message. Speaking in front of others. Saying something out loud. Meeting up with someone.

Before doing them, something shows up inside: tension, a quickening, a discomfort that's hard to explain. And at the same time, a mental clarity: "it isn't a big deal," "it shouldn't be hard for me," "it doesn't make sense to feel this way."

And still, you don't do it. Or you do it with effort, anticipating the end. Or you simply avoid it. And little by little, without realising it, your life starts organising itself around that.

What you feel makes sense, even if it doesn't seem so

Before going further, there's something worth saying. Carrying this for a long time has a cost. Not only the cost of the situations you avoid. Also the cost of explaining to yourself why something happens to you that others seem to do effortlessly. Of trying, not making it, and drawing conclusions about what that says about you. That is tiring. And it makes sense that it tires you.

What you feel isn't a sign that something is broken in you. It's a sign that, with your experience, your organism learned to respond in a way that, at some point, had a logic. A real function: to protect you from something that back then really was threatening, or was at least lived as such.

The problem isn't that this learning existed. The problem is that it generalised: now it activates in situations that don't need it, with the same intensity as before. You aren't too sensitive. You are someone whose organism learned a lesson too well that no longer applies.

That doesn't change by telling yourself you shouldn't feel it. But it can change if you start relating to it in a different way.

It isn't a problem of understanding it better

It isn't that you don't understand. You can reason perfectly well that there's no real danger. You can explain it. You can see it clearly. And yet, in the moment, you do something else.

This usually leads to a hard conclusion: "Then there must be something wrong with me." But if you observe what's happening more closely, you'll see something different: you aren't failing. You're responding to how your experience is functioning in that moment. And that doesn't change with more arguments.

The point where trying to control it stops helping

You've probably already tried this: calming down before speaking, convincing yourself nothing's going on, waiting until you feel ready, avoiding until "it goes away." And sometimes it works. In the short term.

But if you look at the whole picture, a necessary question shows up: is that bringing you closer to the life you want to live? Because the more you need to be calm in order to act, the less you act. And the more you avoid what activates you, the more weight it has next time.

The key recognition

Continuing to try to control this before acting leaves you trapped in the same wait. That recognition isn't giving up. It's no longer insisting on a solution that isn't working. It's what in the first article of this series we called creative hopelessness: the point at which you stop spending energy in a direction that leads nowhere and you can start looking somewhere else.

How the loop is organised

In these situations, something very fast happens:

Anxiety shows up → thoughts show up ("don't do it," "you'll embarrass yourself," "get out of here") → an urge to leave shows up → you avoid, you withdraw, you postpone → there's relief → the cycle repeats.

You don't need to fully believe those thoughts. It's enough that they function as signals. And the relief that follows reinforces exactly that pattern. Not because it's what you want, but because it reduces what you feel.

✦ What matters to recognise

This loop says nothing about your character. It says something about how patterns of behaviour are maintained: through the immediate relief that avoiding produces. Anyone, in the right circumstances, can get caught in a loop like this. And many people are, in different areas of their lives, without knowing it.

Recognising it doesn't resolve it, but it's the only point from which something can be changed.

Introducing a minimal difference

When "I can't do it" shows up, you already know the move: "Right here and now, I'm having the thought that I can't do it." That is defusion. It doesn't change what you feel. It changes how it influences what you do. That thought stops being an automatic command. And a small margin appears.

If you stop for a second right in that moment — before avoiding, before withdrawing — you can access that perspective we've already described in the previous articles: the place from which you notice all of that is happening. Anxiety rises and falls. Thoughts change. The urge to leave comes and goes. But you, from there, can notice: "There is tension now." "Right now there's an urge not to do it." "Right now, this is very uncomfortable." Without becoming that. And from that place — self-as-context — something happens that changes everything: you can choose where to move, even if what you feel hasn't changed at all.

The question that opens movement

In that moment, the question changes. It's no longer "how do I do this without feeling this?" because that returns you to the same loop. The one that opens something different is: "If this weren't deciding for me, what would I do now?" Not perfect. Not complete. Just the next thing.

For that question to have an answer, you don't need great clarity. Is there something in this situation that has to do with how you want to relate to others? With something you value about yourself, even if right now you can't show it? With not letting fear end up deciding for you who you are? That is already direction enough to take a step.

Acting when it isn't easy

There's a very widespread rule: "I have to feel safe in order to do it." But in practice, it works the other way around: when you do something in that direction, even if it's hard, that's when something starts to change. Not before. That's why the move isn't to wait for anxiety to drop. It's to do something with it present.

It's worth clarifying what that means: it isn't enduring until the distress passes on its own. It isn't a test of resistance. It's doing something that matters — speaking, staying, responding — while anxiety is still there, without that anxiety being the one that decides whether you do it or not. That is acceptance in the precise sense: not eliminating what's there, but making room so that you can act in the direction of what matters.

What changes with time isn't that anxiety disappears before acting. It's that you demonstrate, again and again, that you can move even when it's present. And that transforms the relationship you have with it: not because you've eliminated it, but because you've acted despite it, in the direction of something that matters to you.

Not everything that makes you feel good brings you closer

There are decisions that reduce discomfort, and there are decisions that expand your life. Sometimes they coincide, but often they don't. Avoiding can relieve you, but it can also shrink your world. Approaching can be uncomfortable, but it can also open it up.

If the criterion is feeling better, your behaviour will be limited. If the criterion is moving forward in what matters, your behaviour can expand, even if it isn't always pleasant.

How you treat yourself when you don't manage it

When you don't manage it — when you avoid, when you leave, when you don't do what you wanted — the different move isn't to demand less of yourself. It's not to add more weight: "This is being hard." "It makes sense that it's costly." "I can try again." And from there, be able to keep going.

What starts to change

When these moves are repeated, even in an imperfect way, you avoid less, you stay more, you participate more. Your world expands. Anxiety may keep showing up with similar intensity in similar situations. What changes isn't that it disappears. It's what happens in you when it's present. And that, with time, is enough for your life to open up.

You don't need to eliminate it. You need something more precise: not letting it decide for you.

Closing the trilogy

Throughout these three texts on psychological flexibility there's a common thread. When everything switches off and it's hard to get going. When everything weighs and life narrows. When everything activates and anticipation rules. In all three cases, the pattern is the same: trying to feel good before living ends up limiting your life.

And the alternative is also the same in all three: not organising your life around controlling what you feel, but around what makes it worth living.

It's built like this, again and again: notice · let be · return · choose · act

Not as perfection. As practice. It isn't easier. But it is broader, more flexible and more real.

And from there, even if not everything is in order, even if not everything is comfortable, even if not everything is resolved, your life stops shrinking. And it starts, once again, to open up.

Three texts, one single person

You may have recognised things from all three as you read them. That sometimes what predominates is the tiredness and the life that narrows. Other times, the activation and the anticipation. And other times, simply, the feeling that something stops you even though you can't quite explain what.

That is to be expected. These aren't three different types of people. They are three forms in which the same pattern can appear, at different moments or even mixed together, in the same person. The work isn't to identify which one you fit into definitively. It's to recognise when it's happening, in whatever form, and to be able to choose from there.

About the sequence

One last thing about the sequence that appears at the end of each text — notice, let be, return, choose, act —: it isn't a recipe with steps in order. Sometimes you act before you've finished noticing. Sometimes you return to the present moment after having avoided for days. Sometimes letting be arrives when you already thought you had failed.

What matters isn't following it in that order. It's that those moves keep appearing, in an imperfect and repeated way, in the direction of what matters to you.

The real work

Reading about this has a real limit, and it's worth naming it without going around it. You can recognise yourself in every paragraph, understand the mechanism, see the pattern clearly. And in the moment when anxiety shows up, or tiredness, or the urge to avoid, it's possible that everything you've read will stay on the sidelines. Not because you haven't understood. But because understanding isn't enough to change the function of the experience.

That work happens in practice, repeated, and is generally done better with someone alongside you. Not because you can't do it alone. But because there are things that can only be seen from outside, and there are moves that are sustained better when someone is there to see them with you.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) isn't a set of techniques to feel better. It's a learning process that happens in relationship, with someone who can see what you can't see from inside.

If something of what you've read in this trilogy resonates with your experience, that's the moment to take the step. Not when you feel ready. Now, from where you are. Not to resolve this before living. But to learn to live while you resolve it.
