You are not failing: the environment, distress, and what no one asks

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 12 min read

← Back to blog

This is the third article in the series Co-sleeping — Parenting with direction. In the previous articles we explained what conscious co-sleeping is and what happens in each stage of the first three years. Here we talk about the caregiver: their environment, their distress, and something almost no one names.

There are nights when everything works. And nights when nothing works. Nights when the caregiver loses their patience, responds harshly, or simply can't take any more. And the next day comes the question that weighs more than the tiredness: have I hurt them?

This article is for those nights. And for the morning after.

The environment: a decisive factor

There are nights when a caregiver loses patience. When they respond harshly, or do not respond, or simply cannot take any more. And the next day comes the guilt: did I hurt them? Was that one moment enough to break something?

If you recognise those nights, if you know exactly what these lines are talking about, that does not make you a bad mother. It makes you a person caring for another human being with all their resources, including the days when those resources are almost empty. That is also part of the history you build with your child.

You are not the mother who failed last night. You are the one reading this today. Those two things do not contradict each other: they live in the same person who tries, gets tired, and tries again. That is what defines you, not the moment when you couldn't take any more.

The answer, from Contextual Behavioral Science (CBS), is clear and worth saying without softening: no. What shapes the child's repertoire — the set of responses they have available — is not any isolated episode of caregiver dysregulation — inevitable, human, and everyday — but the patterns established over time. A caregiver who gets irritated, tired, or disconnects in moments of high demand is not harming their child: they are being a human being in a demanding context. What matters functionally is the overall trend of the interaction, not perfection in each moment.

From Process-Based Therapy (PBT) — an approach that, instead of applying a general protocol based on diagnosis, intervenes on the specific psychological processes maintaining the problem in this person and at this moment — assessing the family environment is not about identifying parenting deficits or building narratives of harm. It is about detecting the processes that, consistently and over time, may be reducing the bond's flexibility.

Technical note Verbal rules and cognitive fusion: how «I should» becomes a trap

What kind of processes? For example: the caregiver getting trapped in rigid rules about how parenting «should» be. Verbal rules are sentences we have learned that organise what we do, sometimes without our noticing: «if I don't do it perfectly, I'm failing them», «a good mother doesn't lose her patience», «I should enjoy every moment». When those rules act as automatic orders we obey without questioning — what in ACT is called cognitive fusion — they paradoxically increase dysregulation by generating guilt and self-criticism. Another frequent process: losing contact with what really matters to us as a parent in moments of greatest demand. And another: avoiding situations of the child's distress, depriving them of the opportunities for gradual regulation they need to grow.

Emotional overload in babies happens when they are exposed in a sustained way to stimuli — any event, internal or external, that has a function in the baby's behavior: a sound, a light, an abrupt change in routine — that exceed their processing capacity: environments with very intense stimulation without rest periods, abrupt and unpredictable changes in routine. Offering an environment that is sufficiently predictable and respecting the baby's rhythms does not require perfection; it requires an overall trend toward consistency, compatible with all the variability and imperfection that are part of any real family.

What no one usually asks

There is something articles on co-sleeping almost never say: that sometimes the baby in the bed is not only a parenting decision but also the silent symptom of something happening between the adults. That the bed shared with the child can be, without anyone having decided so exactly, the place where the couple has stopped meeting. That there are mothers who practise co-sleeping alone — even with the father at home — because the distance between them already existed before the baby and the baby simply made it visible. Naming this is not an accusation. It is acknowledging that the context in which a child is raised also includes what happens between their caregivers: the tension, the silenced reproach, the desire for connection that finds no moment, the guilt of not being enough on any front at the same time.

From contextual psychology, that context matters — not to judge it, but because it is part of the environment in which the child grows and in which you give care. A mother going through depression, or grief, or a couple's crisis, is not failing in her role: she is being a complete human being in a very hard moment. And that mother — precisely that one — deserves someone to ask how she is doing, not only how the baby is sleeping.

The child's distress: worry or opportunity

When the baby cries and the caregiver does not intervene immediately, something tightens in that adult. The instinct is to come, soothe, solve. And yet, there is a crucial difference between distress that overwhelms the child — and that requires immediate presence — and a manageable discomfort that, if held with calm from nearby, becomes an opportunity. Contextual psychology does not propose ignoring the child's distress; it proposes learning to distinguish when to relieve it and when to accompany it without erasing it — because it is precisely in that second scenario that the child learns that distress is temporary, manageable, and not a signal of danger or abandonment.

Children with a history of consistent contingencies — predictable and sensitive caregiver responses — are better prepared to face frustration. Why? Because they have learned, through differentiated caregiver responses — sometimes consoling, sometimes accompanying without intervening, sometimes waiting — that experiences of distress are events that pass, not signals of danger or abandonment, and that their caregivers will be available to help when needed. This history allows them to explore the world more fully.

Why varying the response matters

It is important that caregivers vary their responses to the child's distress, alternating between physical comfort, verbal comfort, and modifying the context, and allowing the child to experience manageable frustrations. If the caregiver always responds in the same way — for example, always picking up — the child only learns to regulate that way. And if the caregiver sometimes responds and sometimes does not, in an unpredictable way, an escalation pattern can be inadvertently trained: the child learns that they have to cry louder or for longer to get a response. What in this approach is called intermittent extinction — responding inconsistently to a behavior — can consolidate exactly what the caregiver wants to avoid. The alternative is responding consistently but with variety: sometimes with contact, sometimes with voice, sometimes by changing the environment. That allows the child to generalise their regulatory capacity to different contexts and modalities.

Letting the child wait a little while the caregiver is present and available is the first step toward more complex processes, such as reflective thinking and judgment. The key lies in teaching the child to make room for small periods of discomfort — to expand what we might call their window of experiential acceptance: distress is something transitory and manageable, not a threat to be eliminated immediately.

And you also deserve that: not eliminating your own distress immediately, but learning to hold it. The mother who tolerates her own uncertainty without giving up is, without knowing it, the one teaching the most.

Compassionate emotional validation

✦ At two in the morning

A baby cries at two in the morning. The caregiver, instead of turning on the light and picking them up immediately, speaks: «I'm here. I'm with you. It's okay.» With a calm tone, no rush, no urgency. Sometimes that's enough. And when it is, something more important than it appears is happening: the baby is not learning to calm down alone — they are learning to calm down through the caregiver's voice, which is the first step toward calming down through their own memory of that voice.

That is compassionate emotional validation: recognising and naming what the child feels without rushing to remove it.

This process is especially relevant because the caregiver's verbal response — naming, validating, contextualising the emotion — gradually builds in the child perspective frames (I/YOU): the capacity to distinguish their own experience from the other's. That capacity is the basis of empathy and of what in ACT is called self-as-context.

In the context of co-sleeping, this takes shape as auditory-verbal comfort: using a calm and reassuring tone of voice to validate the child's feelings. Young babies do not yet understand the meaning of words. What reaches them is the tone — the paraverbal component — which directly activates the baby's physiological regulation systems before the symbolic function of language is operational. Simple phrases like «I'm here with you» or «we'll always be near you» allow the child to begin building frames of coordination between «caregiver's voice» and «safety,» with the capacity to activate progressively in deferred form — when the caregiver is not physically present — as temporal deictic frames consolidate.

By choosing a verbal response over a physical intervention, caregivers allow the child to begin developing their capacity to calm down through verbally mediated regulation — the move from physical contact to language as a source of calm. As the child grows, simple distress-management tools can be introduced — such as deep breathing or identifying emotions — whose goal is not to eliminate distress but to expand the child's repertoire to relate to it with greater psychological flexibility.
