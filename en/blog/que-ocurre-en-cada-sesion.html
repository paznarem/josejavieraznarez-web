Living what matters

What happens in each contextual psychotherapy session?

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 19 min read

← Back to blog

If you're reading this text, it's very likely that you've been feeling for a while that something keeps repeating. That you're not quite moving forward. That the difficulties wear you out more than you'd like. Maybe you've tried strategies, advice, readings, or even previous therapies, and you still feel like you can't get unstuck.

This page is written for you: to help you understand how contextual psychotherapy works, what sessions with me look like, and how, from there, you can start building a life more aligned with what truly matters.

A place where what you feel is respected

Contextual psychotherapy doesn't try to "fix" you. It doesn't try to "take away" your problems, or turn you into another person. What it does try to do is offer you a space where what you feel, think, and do makes sense. Even when it hurts.

You don't judge your tiredness, your fear, your guilt, or your confusion; and here, it isn't about judging them either. It's about understanding them, locating them in the moment they appear, and, from there, choosing how you want to keep living.

This way of working is grounded in Contextual Behavioral Science (CBS): an approach within scientific psychology that studies human behaviour always in relation to the context in which it occurs. It doesn't just focus on what you feel, but on what function what you feel has in what you do. And from there, it aims to strengthen what allows you to live with more flexibility and more coherence with what matters to you.

The premise of each session

What happens inside you is real. What happens in your environment also matters. And you can always learn to respond differently when that appears, even if you can't always control what occurs.

Beyond that, in every session I work with something that goes further than symptoms: the validation of your own experience. Before any analysis or tool, I acknowledge that what you've lived through has its own logic. That your tiredness isn't weakness. That your blocks aren't failures. It isn't about feeling better in order to start living. It's about starting to live, even if you don't yet feel entirely well.

A session, step by step

Imagine you walk into a session. There are no expectations, and there's no need to make a "good impression." It isn't about appearing brave, nor about justifying your suffering. It's about settling into that moment, just as you are, and starting to observe what's happening. I guide you, but you are the one at the centre.

1. Observing what's happening, without judgement

The first task isn't to "solve." It's to observe. We look at three levels:

  • What's happening inside: thoughts, emotions, impulses, physical sensations.
  • What's happening outside: contexts, relationships, demands, pressures.
  • What you do in each situation: visible and invisible behaviours.

Over time, many people discover that they don't know themselves in the detail they'd need. They feel that they "always freeze," "always fall apart," "always give up" — but they don't stop to see what happens one second before that block. In contextual psychotherapy, that second gets expanded:

  • What thought appears just before you stop trying?
  • What bodily sensation "cuts you off at the knees"?
  • What context makes you feel most vulnerable?

What surfaces is identified, described in detail, and analysed in terms of how it influences what you do. This process allows you to recognise patterns that previously went unnoticed.

There's a moment in this work when something shifts. You realise you can notice what you think, what you feel, what happens in your body, without getting trapped in it. Not because you're special. But because that distance can be trained.

That — placing yourself as an observer of your own experience, distinguishing the you that observes from the content being observed — is called deictic perspective. It sounds technical, but it's something you already do sometimes without realising: when you say "a part of me knows this isn't so serious," you're taking distance from your own experience. What we do in sessions is train that deliberately.

"I'm having this thought" is not the same as "I am this thought." That subtle but decisive difference is one of the pillars of therapeutic work.

This training draws on Relational Frame Theory (RFT). The core idea is this: words aren't just words. Think about what happens when you hear the word "lemon." Your mouth may water, or you may remember a taste. No one has put a lemon in front of you, but your body responds almost as if it had. That happens because your mind has learned to connect the word with the experience. Those connections form networks of meaning that sometimes expand your possibilities and, other times, limit you without your realising it.

Observing all of this calmly, without judging it, is the first step. It isn't about "discovering the truth about yourself." It's about detecting patterns you haven't been aware of until now.

2. Private events: thoughts, emotions, and sensations

Countless things happen inside you:

  • Intrusive thoughts, self-criticism, fears, doubts.
  • Intense emotions: fear, sadness, anger, shame.
  • Impulses: to shout, flee, cancel, withdraw.
  • Physical sensations: a knot in the stomach, palpitations, tension, dizziness, extreme tiredness.

Very often, these experiences are perceived as internal enemies. "I shouldn't feel this." "If I feel this, I won't be able to do what I set out to do." But in contextual psychotherapy, the question shifts focus:

  • Not so much "how do I get rid of this?" but "what function does this serve?"
  • What makes you stop?
  • What protects you, even if in the long run it takes you away from what you want?

The central idea is this: you don't need to control or eliminate what you feel in order to move forward. What you can learn is to relate differently to those experiences. To hold them without fleeing. To observe their function. And to choose an action coherent with what matters to you, even if the distress is still there.

In sessions we practise:

  • Holding the discomfort without fighting against it.
  • Experimenting with small behaviours that hold even when distress is present.
  • Observing how avoidance gives momentary relief, but reinforces fear.
Psychological flexibility

The capacity to keep choosing what matters to you, even when what you think and feel isn't on your side. Not when you're ready. Now.

3. Language, mind, and self-criticism

Language is one of the most powerful tools, and also one of the most dangerous. Thanks to it you can think, plan, imagine, remember. But you can also create stories that keep you trapped:

  • "I'm never enough."
  • "If I fail, I'm worthless."
  • "If I feel this, it means I'm weak."
  • "If I think that, there's something wrong with me."

These phrases aren't just empty words. They are verbal rules: learned sentences that organise what you do, sometimes without you realising it. When taken literally, they work like commands. And they can end up directing your behaviour.

In sessions I work with what you say to yourself: thoughts, reasoning, rumination, self-criticism, memories, beliefs, labels. I help you see them as products of language. Not as absolute truths.

What we work on is:

  • Distinguishing between what a thought says and what that thought does in your behaviour.
  • Seeing how certain self-criticisms protect you from shame, but take you away from action.
  • Learning to observe thoughts as events that happen, not as commands to be obeyed. This is called cognitive defusion.

For example, instead of forcing yourself to "think better" or "not think about that," the session invites you to experiment:

  • What difference does it make to hold the same self-criticism from "I'm having this thought" versus "I am this thought"?
  • What happens if you let the thought be there, but you keep choosing a small action, even when you don't feel ready?

Over time, that rigid image of yourself that you've built up from judgements, labels, and what you believe others think of you — what in this approach is called the conceptualized self — loses its hold over what you do. It isn't about denying what you think. It's about expanding your options for responding.

4. The body and what is felt

Your body isn't just a stage. It's an indicator. Often, emotions and thoughts are connected to specific physical sensations:

  • Pressure in the chest, as if you can't quite breathe.
  • A knot in the throat, difficulty speaking.
  • Lower-back tension, chronic headaches, tightening of the stomach.
  • Trembling limbs.
  • Unexpected fatigue, insomnia, difficulty digesting.

Each sensation is real. It isn't "imagination." And in many people, that physical sensation is experienced as a signal of internal danger. The body seems to say "you can't," and that influences what you do.

Contextual psychotherapy works with that sensation differently:

  • First, validating it: recognising that it is real.
  • Then, locating it: seeing what thoughts and emotions come with it.
  • And then, practising with it: what happens if you sit with that sensation, observe it, and still take a small step?

By learning to hold bodily discomfort without fighting it, many people discover something: that they can act even when they don't feel "well." This doesn't deny or trivialise the pain. What it does is prevent the sensation from becoming an absolute command: "I can't do anything while I feel like this."

5. Context and external pressures

What you feel doesn't happen in a vacuum. It happens in a context: family, partner, children, work, studies, obligations, social norms, constant comparisons.

Many people discover that much of their distress comes from feeling trapped between what they want and what they believe they must do. In sessions we work by observing how that context influences your decisions:

  • What situations reinforce that you avoid certain things?
  • What consequences — real or anticipated — hold you back?
  • What expectations lead you to pretend, to overload yourself, to not say what you feel?

With that perspective, we start to distinguish between what you do out of habit or pressure, and what you choose because it aligns with what matters to you. It isn't about breaking all the rules or harming anyone. It's about learning to move through your context with more flexibility and more awareness.

Beyond that, I pay attention to what happens in our own therapeutic relationship. The patterns that appear between you and me — how you relate, what you avoid, what's hard for you — are opportunities to observe your usual ways of responding and to practise new ones. The session becomes a space where that happens in real time.

6. Values, not just goals

In many cases, motivation runs out because only goals are worked with: "I want to feel better." "I want the anxiety to pass." "I want everything to be sorted out."

But goals on their own don't sustain the effort when the path gets hard. In contextual psychotherapy, we work with values: not goals that are reached and completed, but directions you choose for your life that continue to make sense even if you don't obtain the outcome. They are what orients how you want to live.

  • What kind of relationship do you want to maintain with your family, even when there's conflict?
  • What kind of professional do you want to be, even when the work is demanding?
  • What kind of person do you want to be for yourself, even when you feel vulnerable?

To move in that direction, we work with two kinds of objectives. Achievement goals are small, concrete actions you can take today, connected with what you value. Outcome goals are the long-term consequences those actions point towards. The distinction matters: waiting to have the outcome already achieved before starting to act is one of the most common ways avoidance disguises itself as preparation.

In sessions we work with questions like:

  • "If you had to describe your life in a few words, how would you want it to be?"
  • "What actions, however small, bring you closer to that description?"

When distress is intense, when relapse seems inevitable, it's values — not the emotions of the moment — that allow you to keep direction.

7. Progressive learning and commitment

Contextual therapy doesn't work like a switch. It works like learning: progressive, in spiral, adapted to your resources and your moment. In each session, we observe:

  • What worked?
  • What didn't work?
  • What patterns keep repeating?
  • What can be adjusted?

We don't look for the perfect response. We look for a viable alternative. We set specific, achievable objectives that are evaluated and adjusted session by session, making sure they're connected with what matters to you.

And when the process gets complicated — when there are relapses, difficulties, or moments of resistance — we work on conscious persistence: continuing to act without fighting against what you feel or exaggerating it. Learning from each fall.

Self-compassion

Not self-compassion as a soft concession. But as a concrete way of treating yourself with the same disposition with which you'd treat someone you love. Recognising that you're suffering, without amplifying or suppressing it. In this approach, self-compassion competes directly with destructive self-criticism. It doesn't replace it with "everything is fine." It replaces it with "this is hard, and even so I can keep choosing."

And what happens in the first session?

In the first session, the focus is on listening to you. Calmly. Without judging. Without rushing towards solutions.

What happens in the session itself — how you relate, what you avoid, what's hard for you to say — is also part of the work. It isn't about "analysing you," or "labelling you," or looking for someone to blame. It's about understanding your story from your own experience.

I usually begin with very open questions:

  • How do you feel arriving at the session?
  • What have you been looking for over the past months or years?
  • What do you expect from therapy?
  • What has gone well in your life, however small?

With those questions, we start to draw a map: where you feel stuck, what patterns keep repeating, what values are present even if they're hidden beneath tiredness or self-criticism.

From there, we build a first way of understanding which processes are maintaining your difficulty. It isn't a final truth. It's a hypothesis that's revised and adjusted in each session, based on what you observe and live between one and the next.

"What if I've already tried everything?"

It's very common that, when arriving at consultation, a person feels they've already tried everything: previous therapies, readings, courses, workshops, emotion regulation strategies, mindfulness, relaxation techniques. And still, the distress returns.

In contextual psychotherapy, that experience isn't interpreted as "you're not capable" or "you're not enough to change." It's interpreted as data:

  • Which strategies have worked momentarily?
  • Which patterns persist despite the intention to change?
  • Which consequences keep reinforcing the same behaviour, even when they aren't obvious?

Many times, the difficulty isn't in a lack of effort. It's in the direction of that effort. In how one has tried to solve what's happening. In waiting to "not feel it" as a condition for acting.

With me, we work with respect for everything you've already tried. We don't start from scratch. We start from your story, your effort, and your learning, and we give them a new angle: more contextual, more oriented to function, more aligned with your values.

If you want to go further

If you've read this far, it's likely that you're tired of staying the same. But also that a part of you knows something can change, even if you don't quite know how.

Taking the step doesn't mean having everything clear. It means being willing to observe in more detail what's happening to you. To experiment with something different from the constant struggle. To let the discomfort be there, while you choose actions that bring you closer to what truly matters to you.

I don't offer a magic method. I offer a safe space where what you feel isn't judged, but examined. An approach with practical, observable, repeatable strategies. And active support, where your experience, your small steps, and your relapses are part of the path. Not a failure.

You don't need to feel it "perfectly," nor to be entirely convinced. All it takes is one step. However small.
