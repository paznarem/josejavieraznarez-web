Anxiety, stress and competent performance: a functional reframe

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 12 min read

← Back to blog

How to make anxiety and stress accompany a competent musical audition: a functional contextual reading.

This is the aim of the article. It is not to explain why activation sometimes functions as a threat and disorganizes performance, nor how a relationship with anxiety that interferes with performance gets established. That is another article. The aim here is more precise and more demanding: to make clear that anxiety and stress are fully compatible with the psychological flexibility —the capacity to maintain or change what one does according to what matters, even when activation is present— that is needed to play well, to sustain what has been learned, and for an audition to reflect what the musician can do under real performance conditions.

This article has two readers at once and assumes that from the start. The first is the psychologist, coach or trainer who works with musicians: they will find here a map of psychological processes, a foundation in Contextual Behavioral Science (CBS), and a set of proposals they can contrast with their current practice. The second is the musician seeking to understand their own experience: they will find concrete questions, recognizable dialogues, and a different way of understanding what they feel before and during an audition. Where the text becomes more technical, the musician can jump straight to the dialogues. Where it becomes more psychological, the practitioner can linger on the mechanisms. Both paths are organized to reach the same point: that activation —everything that happens in the body when something relevant occurs: the quickened pulse, the short breath, the narrowed attention— does not displace control of behavior away from the music when the situation matters most.

The best-known treatments for anxiety in musicians, focused on reducing what is felt —those based on mindfulness, those that work on how the person relates to their emotions, and those coming from cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)— have shown consistent effects on how the musician feels and how they respond to what they feel. However, they share a premise that, according to CBS principles, interferes with what the musician needs to do: they measure their success by whether there is less activation or whether the content of thoughts changes. The problem isn't that these interventions don't work; it's that the criterion they use to measure whether they work —less anxiety, a different way of thinking— is not the same criterion that determines whether an audition reflects what the musician can do in practice. From CBS, the key change is not one of quantity but of function: not how much activation there is, but what and how that activation organizes in the behavior of this musician, with this instrument, in this situation where they are being evaluated. That change is not cosmetic. It changes what is observed, what is trained, and what is considered a good outcome.

When we put it this way, we stop asking whether the musician arrives calm enough and start asking something much more important: whether, with everything the context adds, they can still make music in the way they have actually learned to make it.

But this framing remains incomplete if we don't introduce some prior conditions that change how the musician responds: psychological flexibility is not a trait the musician "has" or "doesn't have." It is a repertoire that only organizes itself when there is a clear direction to move toward. Without that direction, activation cannot be integrated; it can only be controlled or avoided.

Core principle

Without operational values —real, concrete directions of action connected to what truly matters to the musician—, flexibility is a chimera.

Because flexibility doesn't consist of putting up with anxiety or adapting to what happens, but of maintaining or changing behavior according to what matters while activation is present. And if there is nothing defined as important that can guide what the musician does, activation becomes automatically coordinated, bound, with threat.

At that point, the musician cannot read anxiety as a signal that something matters. They can only read it as a signal of danger.

When musicians have forgotten what they value: three layers of the problem

The first layer is that musicians have spent years in institutions —conservatories, academies, youth orchestras— where the value of music has always been external and evaluated by others. The teacher approves the fingering, the panel scores the intonation, the sound, the rhythm, the expressiveness… the orchestra selects or rejects. In that context, the question "what do you value in this music?" is genuinely strange. Not because the musician is superficial, but because nobody has asked them before in a functional way. What circulates in its place are borrowed phrases: "I want to express myself," "I want to convey emotions," "I want to do justice to the score." Those phrases sound like values but do not function as values in the sense of ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy), because they do not organize behavior. They do not tell the musician what to do when activation rises, when the passage fails, or when the panel stares at them.

The second layer is more technical and requires a distinction the article will use throughout. Values, in the ACT sense, are not aesthetic preferences or technical instructions: they are chosen patterns of action that give meaning to what the musician does regardless of whether the outcome is good or bad. "Differentiating the melodic lines in polyphonic passages" is a technical instruction: correct, relevant, trained. It may be in the service of a value —making the architecture of the work audible because that is what the musician considers musically honest— or it may simply be a rule they learned from the teacher. The difference doesn't depend only on what is said, but on how it influences what is done. When the technical instruction is connected to a real value, the musician can keep acting from it even if the outcome is uncertain, even if the panel doesn't recognize it, even if activation rises. When it is only a learned rule, the pressure of evaluation turns it into one more exam. In musical training, what is almost always trained are goals —passing, winning the seat, not making mistakes— and when they aren't goals, they are well-learned technical rules. Both can be evaluated and have a clear endpoint. Values don't: a musician can play with real presence, in the here and now, in an audition that goes badly technically, and still remain coherent with their values. But if their whole motivation system is organized around goals or around rules without connection to what matters to them deep down, that distinction has no ground to land on.

Critical point

When behavior is organized by goals that are evaluated from outside, activation becomes functionally bound to the outcome. If the outcome is at risk, activation is experienced as threat. No other reading is possible.

The third layer is the most important for connecting values with facilitating anxiety. Musicians often experience anxiety as a signal that something is going wrong, that they are not sufficiently prepared, that the outcome is at risk. That reading turns activation into threat. But activation can also be read as a signal that something matters. And what matters, matters precisely because it is connected to values.

The difference between the two readings isn't just what is thought, but how that influences what is done: it depends on whether or not the musician has concrete directions of action that can organize their behavior in that moment.

A musician who knows their value is to make the honest work of preparation audible can receive anxiety as confirmation that what they are about to do matters, not as a signal of danger. That reconnection —from activation to value, not from activation to threat— is exactly the mechanism that turns stress and anxiety into facilitators. But it only works if the value is real and concrete, not a generic phrase.

From anxiety to repertoire

In music teaching, in the conservatory and in psychological consultation with musicians, an idea keeps being repeated that seems reasonable but leads to framing the problem wrongly: for an audition to go well, the musician should arrive without anxiety or with as little stress as possible.

From contextual psychology, that premise is not necessary. What an audition needs is not an organism with a low level of activation, but an organism able to keep accessing what it has learned in rehearsal when the context changes and starts to function as a situation where one is being evaluated.

And that access doesn't depend on reducing activation, but on the behavior being organized by directions of action stable enough to hold up under pressure.

The central question is therefore not whether the heart beats faster, whether breathing becomes shorter, or whether anticipatory thoughts appear. The central question is another one: whether, under those conditions, the musician's behavior remains governed by the relevant cues of musical performance.

When that happens, activation doesn't disappear. It changes function. It stops competing with performance and starts modulating it.

Three contexts in which activation serves different functions: preparation, performance and anticipation

For the purposes of this article, it is more useful not to speak of "types of anxiety" as if they were separate entities, but of functional contexts in which activation serves different functions.

In some musicians, a more or less stable pattern of response can be observed in situations where they are evaluated: a greater likelihood of activation in contexts where there is observation, judgment or relevant consequences. This pattern is understood here not as an inner trait, but as the result of a learning history: the person has lived through enough evaluative situations for those situations to start triggering activation.

At this level, what is relevant is not to put a label on the musician, but to understand under what conditions activation appears and what functions it has had in their history.

At a second level, there is activation tied to the concrete situation: the moment of the audition, the minutes before, the first bars. Here activation is not a characteristic of the person, but a response that depends on what is happening in that moment: evaluation, uncertainty, importance of what is at stake.

And at a third level, especially relevant for this article, is the activation that appears during preparation: the anticipation of the audition when the musician rehearses changes what they do in the now. Relational Frame Theory (RFT) explains why this happens: the words and ideas we have learned to associate with a situation can trigger the same responses as the actual situation. The audition is not physically present, but it is functionally active through language.

What this means in practice is that the future audition can transform the function of the rehearsal: what was previously practice becomes coordinated with evaluation, outcome or consequence, and that changes the way the musician relates to what they are playing.

These differences are not "types" of anxiety that need different treatments, but different functional configurations in which activation serves different functions.

This article focuses on two of them: activation in preparation and activation in performance. Not to reduce them, but to show how, under certain functional conditions, they can coexist with competent performance and contribute to sustaining it.

The analysis of broader patterns and their learning history belongs to another level of intervention that is not developed here.

What a competent audition means

In this approach, anxiety and stress are not system errors. They are part of the conditions in which an important action takes place. An audition is not a neutral extension of the rehearsal: it is a context where several functional variables change. There are observers, there is uncertainty, there are consequences, there is a history of comparison, there is personal and professional relevance. All of that changes the conditions under which the musician plays. But the conditions changing does not mean that what has been learned has to disappear. Rather, the challenge of competent musical performance is that what has been rehearsed remains accessible in an environment that adds pressure, visibility and evaluation. That is where psychological flexibility becomes a clear reference for action. ACT defines it as the capacity to contact the present more fully and to persist with or change behavior when doing so serves valued ends; translated into the musical field, that means staying attentive to the task, to listening, to adjustment and to the direction of the phrase even when the organism is activated.

It is worth refining further. We are not talking here about "feeling good in order to play well," but about functional relationships between context, repertoire and consequences. A competent audition is one in which, under conditions of evaluation, the capacities learned in rehearsal remain available: distinguishing shades of intonation, patterns of movement, the organization of time, control of breathing, attention to sound and the capacity for fine adjustment. This forces us to frame performance differently. The criterion is not the absence of internal activation. The criterion is that what has been learned remains accessible. The interpretation in an audition is faithful to what the musician knows how to do when activation does not hijack the control of behavior nor displace it away from the music.

Inside and outside the music: control of behavior

That "outside the music" deserves a more precise analysis. In rehearsal, behavior is usually organized by relatively clear variables: intonation, rhythm, articulation, fingering, support, breathing, balance, phrasing, motor memory, listening to one's own sound or that of the ensemble. In the audition, additional sources of control appear: the presence of the panel, the expectation about the outcome, the anticipation of evaluation, the awareness of being observed. What matters is not that those variables outside the music disappear; that would be unrealistic. What matters is that they do not become what dominates performance. A competent audition does not require the musician to stop noticing evaluation. It requires that, even while noticing it, the main control of behavior remains on the relevant musical variables.
