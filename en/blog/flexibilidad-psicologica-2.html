When life shrinks and it feels like there's no way out

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 10 min read

← Back to blog

There are moments when everything becomes smaller. Not necessarily because circumstances have changed. But because, little by little, you're doing less.

Getting up is hard. Deciding is hard. Starting is hard. What used to be everyday — replying to a message, going out, making a call — begins to feel like too much.

And something appears that many people describe like this: "I don't feel like it." "I can't." "I'm not able to right now." And slowly, without noticing, your life keeps narrowing.

Before going on, a distinction that matters: this isn't the same as the tiredness of a difficult week. Nor is it the same as the sadness of a recent loss, which has a clear cause and a recognisable process. What is described here is different: it's when the narrowing has no single cause, when there isn't one specific moment to point to, when you've simply been less, for a while. If that resonates, keep reading.

Important note

When the narrowing is very intense — when you've been unable to do almost anything for a long time, when your body barely responds, when even the smallest thing requires an effort you simply don't have — that level of limitation may need something more than changing the relationship with your experience. It may need a broader assessment, one that also looks at how your body is functioning physically. This text doesn't replace that.

What is happening, without judging it

Before explaining anything, there's something worth saying clearly: what you feel makes sense. It isn't an exaggeration. It isn't weakness. It isn't something you have to justify or resolve for it to be real.

When life narrows in this way, there is a real cost. You notice it in the body, in your thinking, in your drive. And that cost doesn't disappear because someone tells you that you could do things differently. What can change is how you respond to that experience. Not to eliminate it. But so that it stops being the thing that decides for you.

It isn't lack of strength, it's how behaviour gets organised

From the outside, it's sometimes read as lack of motivation. From the inside, it feels different: as if everything required more than you have available. And then you do something completely logical: reduce. You do less. You avoid what costs. You postpone.

And at that point something important happens: in the short term, there is relief. But in the medium term, there is a clear consequence: your world becomes smaller. This isn't a personal defect. It's a pattern of functioning. When behaviour starts to organise itself around avoiding distress, life keeps narrowing without you explicitly deciding it.

✦ The silent rule

Here something tends to install itself that works like a verbal rule — a learned phrase that organises what you do without you realising it: "If I don't have energy, I can't do anything." It seems obvious. But if you watch it in practice, something different happens: it isn't the lack of energy that stops the action, but the rule that without energy you can't act. And that rule, when followed automatically, reduces the available energy even further.

The role of what you tell yourself

At this point, it isn't only what you do that matters. What shows up in your head in the meantime matters too: "There's no point trying." "It won't help." "I'm not who I used to be."

These thoughts aren't the problem in themselves. The problem is how they function. If they operate as literal descriptions of reality — if you follow them as if they were commands, without any distance — they start to direct what you do. That is what, in the first article in this series, we called fusion: when a thought acts on you as an automatic command.

But there's something else worth noticing. Sometimes it isn't only that those thoughts show up. It's that you stay inside them: going round and round about why you're like this, looking for the cause, reviewing what you have or haven't done, analysing whether there's a solution. That repeated turning — what is called rumination — has its own logic: it seems that if you understand the problem well, you'll be able to resolve it. But in practice the opposite tends to happen: it takes up the space you'd need to do something different, and keeps active exactly the experience you'd want to leave.

That those thoughts organise your behaviour says nothing about your intelligence or your willpower. It says something about how language works: words act on us even when we know they're only words. Imagine being in a room with a lot of noise and several conversations at the same time. If someone across the room suddenly says your name, your attention moves there before you've decided to move it. These thoughts work the same way: not because you've chosen them, but because they're there, active.

You didn't choose for it to work this way. But you can learn to relate to it differently.

The loop you don't see from inside

A very powerful cycle forms here:

Distress shows up → thoughts that interpret it show up → you reduce behaviour → there is immediate relief → your life shrinks → distress increases → and the cycle repeats.

You don't need to break it all at once. You need to introduce a difference at a key point.

Creating a small space

When "I can't" or "there's no point" shows up, you can do something minimal: "I'm having the thought that I can't." It isn't to convince yourself of the opposite. It is defusion: separating the thought from the action, so that it stops being an automatic command. That small change doesn't activate you immediately. But it opens something more important: a space in which to choose.

And if you stop for a moment in the middle of that loop, you can access something we already described in the previous article: the perspective from which you notice that all of this is happening. Thoughts change, sensations change, tiredness rises and falls. But the place from which you observe all of that doesn't change. From there you can notice: "There is tiredness." "There is resistance." "There is an urge to quit." Without becoming that. And from there, choosing is possible, even if the experience doesn't change.

The question that moves something

The question is no longer "how do I get out of this?", because that question sends you back into the same loop. The one that opens movement is different: "If this weren't deciding for me, what would I do now, in small?" Not in general. Now. Not perfect. Enough.

Action when there's no drive

This is where the block usually shows up: "I don't have motivation." And that's true. But if you wait to have it, most likely nothing will change. Because in this pattern, waiting for motivation in order to act is like waiting for a muscle to get stronger before starting to use it. It doesn't work in that order. Motivation, when it comes back, tends to be a consequence of having acted, not a precondition.

This doesn't mean it's easy. It means you have more capacity than you feel right now. That's why change doesn't start by feeling differently. It starts by doing something different, even if it's minimal. A message. Getting up. Opening the door. Taking a step. Not because you feel like it, but because it matters.

About what matters

When we talk about what matters, we aren't referring to having a clear life purpose or knowing with certainty where you want to go. Right now it can be something much smaller and much closer: being a little present for someone you care about. Taking up again something that used to be part of who you are. Going out even for a moment. Replying to that message.

You don't need to have everything clear. You only need to find something — even minimal — where moving has a meaning that isn't only about relieving what you feel. That is already a value in the sense that matters here: a chosen direction, not a grand plan.

It's worth not confusing things: living in the direction of what matters isn't the same as feeling good. If the criterion is "feeling good," life will tend to narrow. If the criterion is "doing what matters," life can widen, even if it isn't always pleasant.

What turns this into real change

A single step doesn't change a life. But repeating steps in a direction does. It isn't about doing it perfectly, or doing it always. It's about coming back. Today you do something small. Tomorrow you do it again. Another day you can't. And the next day you return to it. That is what widens your life again. Not the intensity of the change, but its continuity.

✦ What works varies

Some people need to start from the body — moving, going out, changing something physical — before anything moves from within. Some need first to name what is happening to them, put it into words, before being able to do anything with it. And some need a relational context — someone who is there — for any step to be possible. There isn't a single correct order. What matters is finding yours.

How you treat yourself when you don't manage it

When you avoid again, when you don't do what you'd planned, this shows up: "See, you can't." "This doesn't work." If at that moment you attack yourself, you narrow your margin even further. The different move here isn't to cheer yourself on. It is not to block yourself: "This is hard." "It makes sense that it's costly." "I can try again."

Closing

What you describe — that narrowing, that cost, that difficulty in getting started — isn't a sign that something is broken in you. It's a sign that the pattern has been running for a while and needs more than willpower to change.

Change is built like this, again and again: notice · let be · return · choose · act

Not as perfection, but as practice. Little by little, life stops narrowing. And starts, once more, to open up.

If you want to go further

This text describes the way of working of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), an approach within contextual psychology. If you recognise something of your own experience in what you've read, the next step isn't to read more, nor to wait until you feel strong enough to ask for it. It's to do the work with someone who can support you through it.

Not to get out of this before you can live. But to start moving, from where you are now.
