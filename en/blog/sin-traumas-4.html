Emotional reciprocity: what your children learn from how you look at your own parents

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 11 min read

In the previous article we saw what really gets transmitted across generations —response patterns, not harm— and where that transmission gets interrupted: in behavior, not in the story. In this last article in the series, a piece that directly affects that behavior: how you look at your own parents becomes, without your planning it, part of what your children learn about relationships.

There is a movement that occurs naturally in human development, although it does not always happen at the moment it should.

During childhood and adolescence, the direction of the bond between parents and children is necessarily asymmetric. Parents give. Children receive. Not because children are selfish, but because they are in the process of building resources they don't yet have. That asymmetry is not a flaw in the bond: it is its condition of operation at that stage.

But there comes a moment when that direction must begin to change. Not all at once, not completely, but in a real way. The adult child who continues relating to their parents from the position of someone who evaluates what they received, without incorporating the view of what their parents need, what they gave and what they can no longer give, is operating with a map that stopped corresponding to reality long ago.

That mismatch has consequences. For the relationship with one's own parents. And, in a less visible but equally real way, for the parenting that adult child is building with their own children.

What unilateral demand does not see

When an adult evaluates their parents exclusively from what they did not receive, something is systematically left out of the analysis: the cost of what was given.

A father who worked for decades to support a family was not only fulfilling an instrumental function. He was giving time, energy, presence and in many cases health. He was making decisions that limited his own options to broaden those of his children. He was showing up, day after day, in a task no one had taught him to do and for which no course had prepared him.

That has weight. And when the adult child concludes that all of that was insufficient because emotional expression did not have the form they would have needed, they are not making a complete analysis. They are making a partial analysis from a framework with a very specific focus: detecting what was missing.

This does not mean that what was missing did not matter. It means that a reading that only sees what was missing impoverishes the reality of the bond and, above all, impoverishes the relationship that is still possible.

The question that opens possibilities

From contextual therapies, when an adult describes a relationship with their parents organized fundamentally by demand, disappointment or distancing, there is a question that has frequently not yet been formulated.

It is not: what do my parents owe me?

It is not: have they acknowledged the harm they caused me?

It is: am I seeing my parents as people, or only through the responses I remember receiving from them?

That distinction is harder than it seems. Because seeing one's own parents as people implies incorporating into one's view something that during childhood it was not possible to see: that they too were afraid. That they too carried stories they did not choose. That they too arrived at parenthood without knowing exactly how to do it. That they too made mistakes they did not always know how to repair. That they too loved in the ways they had learned, even though those ways did not always match what their children needed.

Seeing all of that is not exculpating. It is not minimizing one's own pain. It is not closing one's eyes to what did do harm.

It is broadening the gaze. And that broadening has a direct consequence for parenting: when a first-time parent can see their own parents as imperfect people who were present, they no longer have to build a parenting that corrects them. And they can begin to build a parenting that is, simply, their own.

Reciprocity is not debt

It's worth making a distinction that is sometimes confused.

Reciprocity ≠ debt

Emotional reciprocity in adulthood does not mean that adult children owe their parents a specific response, nor that gratitude is a moral obligation, nor that pain experienced must be silenced so as not to make others uncomfortable.

It means something simpler and more difficult at the same time: that the adult child can begin to include in their relationship with their parents the question of how they are. What they need. What weighs on them. What it looks like for them to feel seen and accompanied.

That question does not cancel one's own pain. But it places it in a wider context. And in that wider context, sometimes something unexpected happens: the relationship that seemed closed finds a crack through which something new enters.

Not always. Not in every case. There are relationships where harm was real and sustained, and where distance is a functional response that protects. That also exists and deserves respect.

But in many of the cases that come to consultation, the distance is not protecting from real harm. It is protecting from the discomfort of revising an account that has become identity-shaping. And that discomfort, although real, does not justify the cost of keeping closed a relationship that still has possibilities.

What children transmit without knowing it

There is one last element that directly affects parenting.

Children learn to relate to their reference figures by watching how their parents relate to theirs.

A child who grows up watching their parents look at their grandparents with distance, with unresolved demand, with a narrative of harm that organizes every family gathering, is learning something about relationships: that the people who raised you can become a source of problems, that love does not protect from judgment, that imperfection has lasting relational consequences.

That learning is not transmitted with words. It is transmitted with the texture of how grandparents are talked about at home, with the tone that appears before a family visit, with the way the parent manages the discomfort that unresolved bond produces.

And that same child, when they are an adult, will have a model of how one treats those who raised you.

That is why emotional reciprocity in adulthood is not only a matter between parents and adult children. It is part of what gets transmitted to the next generation. And it can be transmitted as closure, as demand and as distance. Or it can be transmitted as breadth, as compassion and as the capacity to see the other beyond the role they occupied in one's own story.

That choice is available. It does not require that the past have been different.

It requires deciding, in the present, what kind of gaze one wants to practice.

What is already available

There is something that occurs in consultation with a frequency that deserves to be named.

A father or a mother describes in detail everything that was missing. The distance of their own father. The demand of their mother. The moments when no one was there, or when the one who was there did not know how. And while they speak, at some point in that conversation, the boy or girl they have at home appears in the account. And then something changes. The tone. The posture. The way that person occupies space.

Because they know. They know exactly how they want to look at that child. They know what they want them to feel when they make a mistake. They know what they want them to hear when they are afraid. They didn't learn it in a book. They know it because they needed it and didn't always have it. And that absence, paradoxically, taught them with precision the shape of what matters.

That is not a wound. It is a resource. One that is already there, before anything has been resolved.

The problem is not that those parents don't know what they want to give. The problem is that the framework from which they are reading their own story tells them that first one has to heal, that first one has to understand, that first one has to reach some place with the past before being able to be available in the present. And while they wait for that place —which has no precise coordinates and to which no analysis fully leads— the child in front of them keeps needing someone to show up.

From Contextual Behavioral Science, the direction is the opposite. Not first resolve, then be present. Rather: be present —with the story on top, with the unresolved on top, with the fear on top— and discover that that presence, repeated, is what changes the relationship with everything else.

Children do not need parents who have arrived. They need parents who are on the way.

And what those parents will transmit, without having planned it, will not be the story they carried. It will be the evidence that one can carry a story and still show up. That the bond does not require perfection to be sustained. That love does not need to have everything resolved in order to be real.

That also gets transmitted across generations. And it is, perhaps, the most valuable thing a parenting can leave behind.
