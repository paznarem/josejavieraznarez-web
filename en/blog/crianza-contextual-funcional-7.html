When other bonds compete with yours: the child between family, peers and the screen

By José Javier Aznárez Barrio 22 min read

← Back to blog

This is the final article in the Contextual Functional Parenting series. Across the previous six, a complete framework has been developed: from the proposal to analyze processes instead of categories, through the workings of the parent-child relational system, the role of language in the construction of the child's self, the psychological processes of the caregiving adult, co-regulation as a mechanism of flexibility transfer, and the processes that sustain the bond over the long term. This final article addresses a reality that parenting models often ignore: the child does not live exclusively in the family. We analyze what happens when other systems compete to organize their behavior and identity, what the family bond can offer that no other system can, and we close with a critical analysis of the cultural discourse on parenting and the implications for intervention.

The child's relational ecology: when other bonds compete with the family one

One of the most frequently ignored aspects in parenting models is that the child does not live exclusively in the family system. From the moment they come into contact with school, peer groups, adult figures outside the home, and digital culture, the child participates simultaneously in multiple environments, each with its own rules, its own implicit values, and its own forms of reward and punishment. These environments are not neutral: they actively compete with the family system to organize the child's behavior, identity, and emotional repertoire.

The distress many parents express in session —«my child is a different person since starting with those friends», «she doesn't tell me anything anymore», «he does what anyone else says except what we say»— is functionally accurate. It is not paranoia or overprotection: it is the perception, often very precise, that another system of rules has acquired more weight than the family one in organizing the child's behavior. Naming this with precision is the first step toward responding usefully.

The child as participant in multiple environments with different rules

From Relational Frame Theory, every environment in which the child participates installs connections —relational frames— about the same themes: their own body, performance, affection, mistake, belonging, identity. A child who at home has learned that mistakes are recoverable —because repair occurs consistently— may enter a classroom where mistakes are met with public ridicule. An adolescent who at home has learned that showing vulnerability is possible may join a peer group where vulnerability is automatically associated with weakness and exclusion.

These networks of connections do not simply cancel each other out. They operate according to context: in the presence of the peer group, the group's frames are the ones organizing behavior; at home, family frames can regain weight. This is why many parents describe a child who «is different depending on where they are»: they are not describing hypocrisy or instability. They are describing how different relational frames are activated in different contexts.

From early on, caregiving figures outside the home —educators, grandparents, coaches— introduce variations on the frames the child has learned in the family. A grandfather who systematically solves all the grandchild's problems before they can attempt them, or an educator who responds with anxiety to any conflict between children, installs contingencies that may differ significantly from those of the family system. This is not pathological: the variability of environments is a condition of development. The problem appears when the incoherence between systems is so pronounced that the child cannot develop a stable frame from which to operate.

Grandparents

Caregiving figures outside the home are not homogeneous contexts from a functional analysis. Grandparents who participate regularly in the grandchild's care operate with a contextual property they do not share with other reference adults: they do not bear the everyday regulatory pressure that characterizes the parental bond.

The father and mother are inside the system of pressure the child inhabits: they are the ones who must hold limits about screen time, remember tomorrow's exam, manage the conflict with a sibling, and evaluate whether the child is developing well. This position, which is the position of direct and responsible care, has a specific functional cost: the parental adult cannot offer the child a space completely free of performance, because their role requires them to evaluate and guide performance.

The grandparent who is outside that everyday management can offer something that parents, with all their love, cannot offer in the same way: a context where the grandchild's worth has no entry price. Not because the grandparent does not care about the grandchild's development, but because it is not their everyday institutional responsibility to ensure it. This difference of position produces a specific relational context in which regulation occurs without the evaluative weight that, inevitably, accompanies the more committed and more present parental bond.

From RFT, the bond with grandparents also offers something that cannot be improvised: historical continuity. A grandparent who has known the grandchild from day one and can say «you've always been this curious» or «when you were four you were already doing that» is offering temporal frames that reinforce the child's identity as continuous and stable, independent of the present difficult episode. This function —anchoring the child in a story of themselves that is not reduced to the current moment of difficulty— is specific to those who have a real history with them and cannot be simulated.

The implications for intervention are direct: when psychological work with a family identifies that grandparents participate regularly in the grandchild's care, including them in the case formulation is not optional. The frames grandparents install about the child —the judgments about their character, the rules about how distress is handled, tolerance or intolerance for mistakes— are an active part of the system in which the child learns. Working with the parents without including the grandparents when they are habitual caregiving figures means intervening on one part of the system and leaving another untouched.

The peer group as a competing relational system

From middle childhood and with special intensity during adolescence, the peer group is the relational system that most efficiently competes with the family one. This happens for precise functional reasons: the group offers immediate and massive reinforcement (acceptance, belonging, validation), operates with very explicit relational rules, and applies social consequences with a speed and intensity that the family system can rarely match.

An adolescent whose peer group has an implicit norm —don't cry, don't show fear, don't admit difficulties— receives a system of rules about emotional expression that may directly conflict with what they have learned at home. If they have learned at home that showing vulnerability is safe and that adults respond with support, they have resources to navigate that tension. If they have also learned at home that emotions are signs of weakness, the group reinforces an already installed pattern and rigidity consolidates.

What the group offers that the family cannot offer in the same way is horizontal belonging: being one among equals, being chosen by those who have no obligation to love them. This need is legitimate and its satisfaction is part of normal development. The functional problem is not that the child seeks to satisfy it: it is when the price of belonging to the group is the complete adoption of frames that contradict values the family has built over years —honesty, respect, self-care— and the child does not have the resources to negotiate that tension.

✦ In session: the fifteen-year-old who «doesn't tell us anything anymore»

A fifteen-year-old who has developed an open communication relationship with her family begins to associate with a group where talking about problems is considered «whiny.» Within a few weeks, the parents notice that «she doesn't tell us anything anymore.»

Functionally, what has happened is that the relational frame of the group («showing problems = being a drag = losing belonging») has acquired more weight than the family frame in the context of social interactions. The solution is not to isolate her from the group: it is to keep the family system as a space where that cost does not exist.

Digital culture as a third bonding system

Digital platforms, content creators, and online communities operate as systems with unique characteristics: they are available at any moment, they offer extremely immediate reinforcement, and they build relational frames on practically every domain relevant to development —the body, academic performance, relationships, gender, politics, identity.

✦ Two hundred hours a year installing frames

A fitness influencer that an adolescent watches for forty minutes a day offers her, over the course of a year, more than two hundred hours of content installing frames about what a female body should weigh, what counts as physical success, and what merits admiration.

No parental message about the body can compete in terms of exposure time with that system. Trying to do so head-on —criticizing the influencer, banning the platform— usually triggers the opposite effect: the family frame becomes associated with restriction and the digital frame becomes associated with freedom.

A content creator who normalizes aggressive language, ridicule as a form of humor, or systematic mistrust toward any figure of authority installs frames that transform the function of the parental figure itself. A parent who used to be «the one who sets limits» can come to be processed as «the enemy» through frames derived from digital consumption. This is not exaggeration: it is the mechanism of transformation of functions described in the third article of this series, operating through a system of extremely high frequency and intensity.

The most frequent parental response to this —access control, banning platforms, content monitoring— has value when content is directly harmful, but as a general strategy it has a clear functional limitation: it does not modify the relational frames the child has already installed, and it can deteriorate the family bond precisely at the moment when that bond is the main source of resources for navigating digital influence.

What the family bond can offer that no other system can

Parents' distress in the face of competition from other systems has a real basis. But the most frequent response —trying to compete on the same ground: being more fun than friends, more permissive than the group, more present than the phone— is doomed to fail because the family system cannot win on that ground. What the family system can offer is something no other system offers, and that turns out to be, functionally, the most valuable.

Unconditional acceptance — no peer group, no digital community, and no school environment offers the certainty that the child's place in the relationship does not depend on their performance, appearance, popularity, or behavior in their worst moment.

Repair without irreversible cost to the bond — the group expels, excludes, or redefines hierarchies. Digital platforms do not repair: they amplify the mistake. The family system, when it works well, offers the repeated experience that the bond survives the worst moments.

Temporal perspective — the family system is the only one that can offer the child a perspective on who they are over time: that what they feel today does not define what they will be, that mistakes have resolution, and that there is an adult who has been betting on them for years.

When parents understand that this is what they can offer —and that trying to compete on the ground of immediacy, entertainment, or absence of limits pushes them away from that function— a significant reordering of priorities tends to occur. The relevant question is not «how do I get my child to prefer being at home rather than with their friends?» but «is the family the place my child returns to when things get complicated outside?»

Parental distress at losing influence

Many parents arrive in session describing the feeling of losing their child. Not in a literal sense, but in a functional one: the child they knew, the one they had contact with, whose values they recognized, seems to have been replaced by someone who obeys other rules and responds to other signals. This experience, though painful, is rarely a sign that something has seriously failed. More often it is a sign that the process of individuation is happening, in a way the family system was not prepared to hold.

Individuation —the process by which the child builds an identity differentiated from the family one— requires, by definition, that the child try out relational frames different from those at home, question rules they previously accepted, and at times act in ways that contradict family values. This is uncomfortable. Sometimes it is alarming. But the fact that it is happening does not mean the family bond has failed: it means it is doing what it should, which is holding a child who is exploring the limits of their identity.

The functionally most relevant question is not «how do I recover the influence I had?» but «when my child has a serious problem —with a friend, with alcohol, with something they have done and regret— who do they turn to?» If the answer is «to me», the family bond is still the system of reference. If the answer is «to anyone but me», it is time to examine what is happening in the family system that has made the cost of turning to it too high.

This has direct implications for work with parents of adolescents: before intervening on the child's behaviors, it is useful to examine whether the bond is still a safe space for the child. An adolescent who knows they can tell their parents something difficult without receiving a reaction of panic, judgment, or immediate control has a resource that no peer group and no digital platform can replicate. Building and maintaining that space is, possibly, the most important parental task during adolescence.

The contemporary cultural discourse on parenting

Contextual psychology offers tools for critically analyzing the contemporary cultural discourse on parenting, which in recent years has acquired specific characteristics that deserve attention.

The popularization of attachment and the installation of rigid rules

The popularization of attachment theory through social networks, digital content platforms, and low-precision science communication has produced a set of widely shared verbal rules that operate as relational frames that transform the function of everyday parental behaviors.

Analysis — Rigid verbal rules that reduce parental flexibility

The rule «if you don't respond immediately to crying, you damage attachment» transforms the function of a delay in response —which under normal conditions can be functionally neutral or even positive for the development of frustration tolerance— into a sign of irreversible damage. When a mother internalizes this rule, the baby's crying acquires functions of extreme urgency that do not correspond to what is actually happening: the result is a state of hypervigilance that exhausts the adult, reduces their margin for flexible response, and, paradoxically, can deteriorate the very quality of co-regulation it intends to protect.

The rule «all childhood trauma comes from the parents» installs a causal frame that turns every difficulty in the child into evidence of parental damage, regardless of the multiplicity of factors that contribute to development. This rule has a particularly destructive effect on parents whose children present difficulties that do not originate in the parental bond —different processing profiles, adverse experiences outside the home, medical conditions— because it assigns them a causal responsibility that does not correspond to functional reality.

The cumulative effect of these rules is the installation of a state of permanent vigilance over one's own parental behavior that has measurable psychological consequences: increased self-criticism, reduced margin of spontaneous response, and avoidance of necessary parental behaviors (setting limits, holding consequences, allowing the child to experience distress) for fear of producing damage.

The confusion between sensitivity and absence of limits

A recent development in cultural discourse that deserves specific analysis is the tendency to equate parental sensitivity with the absence of limits. This confusion, which functional analysis can articulate with precision, produces parenting patterns in which avoidance of conflict is presented as attunement to the child.

Parental sensitivity: a functional definition

Parental sensitivity is the capacity to detect the child's signals, interpret them correctly in the context in which they occur, and respond to them in an attuned way.

Sensitivity does not exclude limits: it includes them as an attuned response when the context requires. A sensitive parent is one who sets a limit when the child needs it, not one who avoids every limit so as not to produce distress.

The confusion between the two concepts has observable functional consequences: it produces family systems where the child has access to abundant emotional validation but scarce behavioral structure. The result is a child who feels heard but who has not developed the resources to tolerate the frustration that limits produce. This combination —high validation without structure— is functionally distinct from classical permissive parenting, because it operates under a sophisticated verbal justification that makes it harder to question.

The psychologization of ordinary relational conflict

The mass access to psychological terminology through digital platforms has produced a phenomenon that contextual psychology can analyze with precision: the transformation of ordinary relational conflicts into clinical categories.

When a parent says that their six-year-old is «gaslighting them» or that their adolescent «has narcissistic traits», they are applying clinical frames to behaviors that may have far simpler functional explanations and that, framed as pathology, receive inadequate responses. A six-year-old who denies having done something they just did is not manipulating: they are avoiding an aversive consequence with the resources they have. An adolescent who does not consider their parents' feelings during a phase of intense individuation is not narcissistic: they are reorganizing their relational frames in a way that, while uncomfortable, is expectable in development.

The psychologization of ordinary conflict has a paradoxical effect: instead of facilitating understanding, it blocks it. When the adult frames the child's behavior as pathology, they lose access to the functional question —«what organizes this behavior in this context?»— and become stuck in the categorial question —«what's wrong with my child?». Returning to functional analysis —what is happening here and now, what function does this behavior serve, what can I change?— tends to produce more relief and more options for action than any diagnostic label.

Implications for intervention: CFP principles in practice

Functional assessment as the first step

Assessment from CFP does not begin with tests or standardized questionnaires. It begins with a precise question: what exactly happens in the moments when the relationship breaks down? This question, formulated with enough specificity, produces clinically more useful information than any personality profile or categorial diagnosis.

Functional assessment seeks to identify: what specific events (internal and external) precede the loss of flexibility in the adult; what responses the adult emits when they lose that flexibility; what function those responses serve (experiential avoidance, cognitive fusion, loss of contact with values); and what consequences those responses have on the relational system in the short and long term.

This assessment is not carried out on the adult as an isolated individual but on the system: it includes the adult's processes, the child's signals that activate those processes, and the contextual conditions (sleep, support, isolation, personal history) that modulate the threshold of flexible response.

In practice, functional assessment produces a map of the system that can be communicated to the adult in precise and non-pathologizing terms: «when your child shouts and you are tired, the thought appears that you are doing this badly, and instead of staying with the discomfort you act so it stops, which works in the short term but teaches something you don't want to teach.» This formulation is more clinically useful than «you have a permissive style» because it points to exactly where, when, and on which process to intervene.

Work with the caregiving adult as a first-order intervention

One of CFP's most important contributions is the proposal that work with the caregiving adult —especially with their own processes of psychological flexibility— is a first-order intervention for the child's socioemotional development. It is not a complement to work with the child: in many cases, it is the intervention with the most functional leverage available.

This does not mean the child does not need direct intervention when there are specific difficulties. It means that modifying the processes that organize the caregiving adult's behavior produces changes in the relational system that the child cannot produce alone, regardless of how much individual work is done with them.

In practice, this means that a consultation about «my child's behavior» can be more effectively resolved by working with the adult who brings the child to consultation than with the child themselves. Not because the child does not have real difficulties, but because the relational context in which those difficulties occur is more modifiable, and with greater impact, through the adult.

Validation as a specific functional intervention

Validation is not a generic empathic gesture or an active-listening technique. It is an intervention with a precise function: recognizing that the child's experience has a comprehensible logic in the context in which it occurs, without that recognition implying approval of the behavior that experience organized.

The central functional distinction is this: validating the experience is not validating the behavior. «I understand you were very angry when they called you that in front of everyone» is not equivalent to «and that's why it's okay that you hit them.» This distinction, which seems trivial when stated like this, turns out to be hard to implement for many adults in situations of high activation, because the automatic tendency under stress is to equate understanding with justifying or with giving in.

Three conditions for effective validation

Specific — names the concrete experience, does not generalize it.

Contextual — connects it to the situation that produces it.

Separate from the limit — it is not mixed with the correction in the same sentence. The functional sequence is always: first validation, then the limit. The order is not protocol: it is the physiological condition for the limit to be processed as information and not as threat.

Conclusions

This article —and the series that precedes it— has proposed the construct of Contextual Functional Parenting as an organizing framework that shifts the analysis of parenting from the level of categories (styles, types of attachment) to the level of functional processes. This shift does not invalidate the contributions of earlier traditions; it proposes a more precise level of analysis, more directly oriented to intervention.

The central principles of CFP can be summarized as follows:

Parenting as a reciprocal system — it cannot be analyzed by separating the adult from the child or the pair from their relational, cultural, and physiological context.

Relational frames transform development — the history of the parent-child bond transforms the function of stimuli, behaviors, and experiences more deeply than any isolated episode.

The adult's psychological flexibility is a contextual condition — the child can inherit it through the history of co-regulation before formal language can do that work directly.

The family bond offers what no other system can — unconditional acceptance, repair after mistakes, and temporal perspective, in the face of the systems that compete to organize the child's behavior and identity.

Effective intervention identifies processes, not categories — it requires the profile of processes with the greatest functional weight in each case, not generic protocols for diagnoses.

Cultural discourse can reduce parental flexibility — some rigid verbal rules about parenting produce hypervigilance, self-criticism, and experiential avoidance with identifiable consequences.

Contextual Functional Parenting does not propose a new school or a new program. It proposes a more precise way of asking the central question of any work with families: what is happening in this relationship, in this moment, and what can be changed from now on?

If reading this series you have recognized yourself, it is possible that you do not need more information or more models. You have probably already made that effort. What usually makes the difference at this point is being able to look with precision at what is happening in your specific case: in which moments the margin is lost, which processes are taking control, and how to intervene there without adding more demand on yourself. That is the work I do in session: not evaluating how you are doing it, but understanding with you what is happening and recovering a way of being with your child that does not depend on everything being easy in order to hold up.

Parenting is not competing with the world that surrounds your child. It is being the place they return to when that world gets complicated. No other system offers that. Only yours.
